## Executive Summary

Keith Johnstone (1933-2023) was a British-Canadian educator, playwright, and theatre director who revolutionized improvisational theatre. He developed a comprehensive system for spontaneous creativity that has applications far beyond the stage—in everyday conversation, storytelling, business, education, and personal growth.

His two foundational books, **"Impro: Improvisation and the Theatre"** (1979) and **"Impro for Storytellers"** (1998), are among the best-selling books ever published on improvisation. His formats—Theatresports, Maestro Impro (Micetro), Gorilla Theatre, and The Life Game—are performed in over 90 countries worldwide.

Johnstone's philosophy centers on the idea that creativity is natural and that education suppresses it. By understanding **status dynamics**, **accepting offers**, **being obvious**, and **reincorporating elements**, anyone can become a compelling spontaneous storyteller.

## 1. BIOGRAPHY AND BACKGROUND

### Early Life and Education

Donald Keith Johnstone was born on February 21, 1933, in Brixham, Devon, England. He grew up hating school, finding that it blunted his imagination and made him feel self-conscious and shy. This experience profoundly shaped his later teaching philosophy.

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Remembering his schooling left Keith believing that the education system was blunting creativity. Keith wrote a list of rules of 'Things teachers stopped me from doing' then he encouraged his students to do the opposite. His unorthodox technique was a success." — ITI Official Website

### Career Development

After attending St Luke's College Exeter, Johnstone taught at a working-class school in Battersea, London in the early 1950s. In 1956, George Devine and Tony Richardson, artistic directors of the Royal Court Theatre, commissioned a play from him. He subsequently became Associate Artistic Director, working as a play-reader and director.

From 1973 to 1975, he was a professor at Queen's University in Kingston, Ontario, Canada. In 1977, he produced the first Theatresports competition in Calgary, Alberta.

**Key Dates:**
- 1933: Born in Brixham, Devon, England
- 1950s: Teaching in London, begins Royal Court Theatre work
- 1956: Commissioned by Royal Court Theatre
- 1970s: Develops Theatresports concept
- 1977: First Theatresports competition in Calgary
- 1979: Publishes "Impro: Improvisation and the Theatre"
- 1998: Publishes "Impro for Storytellers"
- 2023 (March 11): Died at age 90

### Teaching Philosophy

Johnstone's teaching was a deliberate reversal of his own education. As Mark Ravenhill wrote in The Guardian (2009): "Whereas his teachers told him to think more, he'll tell his students to think less. He was advised to plan a story before it was written. Now he says: listen to an audience and let the tale tell itself."

**Characteristic Phrases:**
- "Be obvious."
- "Please, don't do your best."
- "Be average."
- "Screw up and look happy."
- "What comes next?"

## 2. CORE PHILOSOPHY: SPONTANEITY AND CREATIVITY

### The Problem with Education

Johnstone believed that conventional education systematically destroys natural creativity and spontaneity.

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "In a normal education everything is designed to suppress spontaneity, but I wanted to develop it." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Many teachers think of children as immature adults. It might lead to better and more 'respectful' teaching, if we thought of adults as atrophied children." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

### The Nature of Spontaneity

Spontaneity is not randomness—it's accessing your authentic self before social conditioning filters it.

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "When you act or speak spontaneously, you reveal your real self, as opposed to the self you've been trained to present." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "You are not imaginatively impotent until you are dead. You are only frozen up." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

### The Inner Censor

One of Johnstone's key insights was identifying the "censor"—the internal voice that blocks spontaneous expression.

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "One way to bypass the censor who holds our spontaneity in check is to distract him, or overload him." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

Example exercise: Write out a paragraph on paper (without premeditation) while counting backwards aloud from a hundred. The counting occupies the censor, allowing spontaneous content to emerge.

## 3. THE "YES, AND" PRINCIPLE: ACCEPTING OFFERS

### Understanding Offers

Johnstone calls anything that an actor (or person) does an "offer." Every action, word, gesture, or suggestion is an offer that can either be accepted or blocked.

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "A block is anything that prevents the action from developing, or that wipes out your partner's premise. If it develops the action it isn't a block. Bad improvisers block action, often with a high degree of skill. Good improvisers develop action." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

### The Yes-Sayers vs. No-Sayers

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "There are people who prefer to say 'Yes', and there are people who prefer to say 'No'. Those who say 'Yes' are rewarded by the adventures they have, and those who say 'No' are rewarded by the safety they attain. There are far more 'No' sayers around than 'Yes' sayers, but you can train one type to behave like the other." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

### Why Acceptance Works

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Good improvisers seem telepathic; everything looks pre-arranged. This is because they accept all offers made—which is something no 'normal' person would do." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

### Making Interesting Offers

Not all offers are equal. Johnstone distinguishes between dull and interesting offers:

**SOURCE (SECONDARY):** "Learn to make interesting offers—'Good morning!' is dull, but 'Good morning, great heavens Frank, did they let you out?' is interesting." — Impro summary

### Overaccepting: The "It's Tuesday" Technique

Overaccepting is treating a mundane offer as if it were extraordinary:

**Example:** Someone says "It's Tuesday." Instead of a flat response, overaccept: "Oh my God, that's when my brain surgery is scheduled!"

### The "Yes, But" Variation

Accept, then add a "but" clause:
- "Is that your dog?"
- "Yes, but I'm thinking of selling him."
- "Will you sell him to me?"
- "Yes, but he's expensive."

This keeps the scene moving while adding complications.

## 4. STATUS: THE HIDDEN DYNAMICS OF INTERACTION

### What is Status?

Status is Johnstone's most influential concept. It refers not to social standing, but to behavioral dominance in any given interaction.

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Johnstone understands status as something one does, independent of the social status one has. There is no neutral status, but there is always a gradient." — Summary of Johnstone's work

**Key Insight:** Status is performed moment-to-moment through behavior, not determined by external factors.

### High Status vs. Low Status Behaviors

**High Status Characteristics:**
- Less movement, especially of the head
- Relaxed posture (the "cherub posture")
- Stillness and deliberate movement
- Taking up space
- Speaking slowly
- Direct eye contact
- Finishing sentences without trailing off

**Low Status Characteristics:**
- More movement, fidgeting
- The "fear crouch" posture tendency
- Reducing physical space occupied
- Speaking quickly
- Breaking eye contact
- Not finishing thoughts
- Apologizing, hedging

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Less movement indicates higher-status (especially of head), and there is no action that is done which doesn't indicate/raise/lower status." — Keith Johnstone

### Status Transactions

Status is constantly being negotiated in conversation:

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Many of our behaviors are driven by our desire to achieve a particular level of status relative to those around us. People are continually raising and lowering their status in conversation through body language and words." — Summary of Johnstone's work

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Keith Johnstone used the image of a state change, which can be moved in two ways. Either I diminish the status of the other ('you stink'), or I exalt my own ('I smell good')." — Johnstone analysis

### Status and Friendship

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Johnstone was asked where friendships fall into this. Surely friends wouldn't do this kind of thing to one another. Actually, friends do it the most. In this context, we define a friendship as a group of two or more who have unconsciously agreed to play 'status games' with each other." — Analysis of Johnstone

### The Discovery of Status

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "In 1963, Keith Johnstone observed that actors at the Royal Court Theatre Studio struggled to mimic authentic, ordinary conversations. They found talky scenes dull and focused on generating interesting ideas, rather than allowing natural interaction." — Psychology Today

Johnstone asked his actors to try to seem very slightly higher or lower in status than their partner. "The scenes became 'authentic', and actors seemed marvellously observant. Suddenly we understood that every inflection and movement implies a status."

### Status and Blocking

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "There is a link with status transactions here, since low-status players tend to accept, and high-status players to block. High-status players will block any action unless they feel they can control it." — Johnstone analysis

## 5. BEING OBVIOUS: THE PARADOX OF ORIGINALITY

### The Core Teaching

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "The improviser has to realise that the more obvious he is, the more original he appears. I constantly point out how much the audience like someone who is direct, and how they always laugh with pleasure at a really 'obvious' idea." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

### Why Trying to Be Clever Fails

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Ordinary people asked to improvise will search for some 'original' idea because they want to be thought clever. People trying to be original always arrive at the same boring old answers." — Keith Johnstone

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "No two people are exactly alike, and the more obvious an improviser is, the more himself he appears. If he wants to impress us with his originality, then he'll search out ideas that are actually commoner and less interesting." — Keith Johnstone

### The Inspired Artist

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "An artist who is inspired is being obvious. He's not making any decisions, he's not weighing one idea against another. He's accepting his first thoughts." — Keith Johnstone

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "If Mozart had tried to be original, it would have been like a man at the North Pole trying to walk north." — Keith Johnstone

### Accepting First Thoughts

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Switch off the no-saying intellect and welcome the unconscious as a friend: it will lead you places you never dreamed of, and produce results more 'original' than anything you could achieve by aiming at originality." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

## 6. NARRATIVE SKILLS: STORYTELLING TECHNIQUES

### Storytelling as Natural Ability

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Johnstone believes that storytelling is an innate human ability, and he introduces various techniques to help people become better storytellers. He emphasizes the importance of listening and responding, and encourages students to trust their instincts when crafting stories." — Impro summary

### The Backwards Improviser

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "The improviser has to be like a man walking backwards. He sees where he has been, but he pays no attention to the future. His story can take him anywhere, but he must still 'balance' it, and give it shape, by remembering incidents that have been shelved and reincorporating them." — Keith Johnstone, "Impro"

### Reincorporation: The Key to Story Structure

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Johnstone advises that an improviser should 'look back when you get stuck, instead of searching forwards. You look for things you've shelved, and then reinclude them.'" — Johnstone summary

**Why It Works:**

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Very often an audience will applaud when earlier material is brought back into the story. They couldn't tell you why they applaud, but the reincorporation gives them pleasure. Sometimes they even cheer! They admire the improviser's grasp, since he not only generates new material, but remembers and makes use of earlier events that the audience itself may have temporarily forgotten." — Johnstone summary

**Example of Reincorporation:**
Walking in a forest → running from a bear → taking a boat to an island → hearing the bear outside → the bear is in the boat → he makes it to the island → killing the bear → wearing his skin...

### Story vs. Sequence of Events

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "A made up sequence of events is storytelling but doesn't constitute a story. There's no natural place for it to stop since there's no re-incorporation going on, no cross-referencing of previously mentioned happenings. This is kind of an improviser's Chekhov's Gun." — Johnstone analysis

### Interrupting Routines: The Easy Way to Generate Stories

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "If he says 'Make up a story', then most people are paralyzed. If he says 'describe a routine and then interrupt it', people see no problem." — Keith Johnstone

**Example of Routine Interruption:**
- Actor goes on stage and starts eating a dinner of shepherd's pie (establishing a routine)
- Actor finds something in the pie (breaking the routine)
- Actor cleans the pie off the thing (routine)
- Actor finishes cleaning it and discovers it is a ring (breaking the routine)
- Actor paces around trying to remember who had a ring like that (routine)

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Once they understand the concept of 'interrupting routines,' then they aren't stuck for ideas anymore." — Keith Johnstone

### Platform and Tilt

**Platform:** The foundation or setup of a scene that establishes normality.

**Tilt:** The thing that disrupts the platform and forces action.

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "The tilt in Keith Johnstone terms is the thing that pushes the platform into action, the balance of normality has been tilted." — Hoopla Improv

**Example:**
- A couple having breakfast together (routine/platform)
- The lady reveals she is pregnant (tilt)
- They discuss how happy they are and plans for the child (new routine)
- The baby says from inside the womb "I want to go to private school" (tilt)

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "To be good at narrative, don't worry about the narrative—just set a firm platform, expand it, and reincorporate constantly. You don't know where you're going, just where you've been, and that's what gives it structure." — Narrative workshop notes

### Circle of Possibilities

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "According to this technique, a scene involves an ever-shrinking 'circle of possibilities', which defines what sorts of offers the improviser might reasonably make in the scene." — Theatresports wiki

## 7. MASKS AND TRANCE

### The Final Chapter of "Impro"

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "In the final chapter of his book 'Impro,' Johnstone addresses the subject of masks. For him, masks and improvisation are intimately connected because both are connected with the possibility of entering quickly and completely into a trance state. It is in this trance state, he suggests, that dimensions of creativity unknown even to the actor themselves emerge." — Johnstone analysis

### The Mask as Tool

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "A Mask is a device for driving the personality out of the body and allowing a spirit to take possession of it." — Keith Johnstone

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "He saw masks as a tool for actors to temporarily shed their personal identities and inhabit new ones more fully, discussing how wearing masks can help actors lose self-consciousness, entering a kind of trance that allows for deeper emotional and physical expression." — Impro summary

### Western Culture's Distrust of Trance

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Johnstone believed that our Western culture doesn't understand Masks because it distrusts trance states, and that education may be seen as primarily an 'anti-trance activity.'" — Johnstone summary

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "We distrust spontaneity, and try to replace it by reason: the Mask was driven out of theater in the same way that improvisation was driven out of music." — Keith Johnstone

## 8. GAMES AND EXERCISES

### Core Training Games

**1. Word at a Time Story**
Two or more improvisers alternate words as they tell a story. One player says the first word, the next says the second word, etc. Can start with "Once, Upon, A, Time..."

**Training Benefits:**
- Being "in the moment" (not planning ahead)
- Listening and being present
- Accepting and building on offers
- Being obvious
- Teamwork and support

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "If you try too hard to be clever or funny in this game it slows down and just makes it hard for each other. But try to be obvious instead and the story will start to tell itself." — Hoopla Improv

**2. Freeze Tag (Spacejump)**
One person performs in the center. At any time, another can say "freeze." The performer stops, another takes their place, starts a new scene justified by the frozen position.

**Training Benefits:**
- Spontaneity
- Accepting/cooperation
- Physical and verbal offers
- Observation skills

**3. Gibberish Expert**
One player is an expert who speaks only in gibberish. Another player translates. Gets players away from speech content and focuses on vocal expressiveness and body language.

**4. Association Games**
- "Tell me a color"
- "Put your hand in an imaginary box, what do you take out?" — "A cricket ball"
- "Unscrew it, what's inside?" — "A medallion"
- "What's written on it?" — "Christmas 1948"

**5. It's Tuesday (Overaccepting)**
Practice treating mundane statements as if they were extraordinary revelations.

**6. Yes, But**
Accept everything, then add a "but" clause that creates complication while moving the scene forward.

**7. Automatic Writing**
Write continuously without stopping to think or edit. Johnstone sometimes had students do this while counting backwards to occupy the "censor."

**8. Status Exercises**
Try to seem very slightly higher or lower in status than your partner. Practice raising and lowering status seamlessly.

**9. Routine Interruption**
Describe a routine, then interrupt it. The interruption creates narrative.

**10. Typing Scene (Typewriter)**
A typist narrates as they mime typing, and improvisers jump in to enact scenes and add developments.

## 9. JOHNSTONE'S FORMATS

### Theatresports

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "Theatresports is a form of improvisational theatre which uses the format of a competition for dramatic effect. Opposing teams can perform scenes based on audience suggestions, with ratings by the audience or by a panel of judges. Developed by director Keith Johnstone in Calgary, Alberta, in 1977, the concept of Theatresports originated in Johnstone's observations of techniques used in professional wrestling to generate Heat, or audience reaction." — Wikipedia

**Philosophy:**
- Corniness and gags lower scene quality
- Emphasis on building characters and spontaneous, collaborative storytelling
- Jokes and gags are seen as disrupting narrative, avoiding collaboration

**Penalties:** Improvisers can be "punished" for breaking rules—acting a scene as a pig, barefoot, wearing a penalty basket, etc.

### Maestro Impro (Micetro)

A competitive format where individual improvisers are scored and eliminated through rounds.

### Gorilla Theatre

Improvisers pitch scene ideas and direct each other, with audience voting on whose scenes they want to see.

### The Life Game

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "The Life Game combines interviews with improvisation to create an electrifying storytelling event. Each episode is based on one person's life story—their successes and failures, hopes and dreams, love and loss—told as a live interview interspersed with improvised scenes." — CBC Radio

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "The Life Game was arguably Keith's favourite format. He protected it as a parent would protect their child." — The Improvisation School

**Creative Direction Examples:**
- For a story about parents divorcing: large drawings of both parents on panels upstage, slowly pulled apart
- For a man who shot himself and has a bullet near his heart: a performer plays the heart, another plays the bullet, standing as close as possible without touching

## 10. APPLICATION TO EVERYDAY LIFE

### Beyond the Stage

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "One reason why Johnstone's book is so interesting is that it focuses on teaching creativity via the medium of physical/embodied expression. Since physical performance is what we as social animals are constantly doing, mostly in public but also in private, the methods in the book have big implications for all of everyday life." — Theory Engine

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "His ideas have been applied in many sectors of life, including management, writing, film-making, teaching, psychology and therapy of various kinds." — Keith Johnstone official site

### For Conversational Storytelling

1. **Accept offers in conversation** - Don't block what others say
2. **Overaccept the mundane** - Make ordinary moments extraordinary
3. **Reincorporate** - Bring back earlier elements for satisfying stories
4. **Play with status** - Adjust status dynamics for different effects
5. **Be obvious** - Your first thought is usually best
6. **Interrupt routines** - Disruption creates narrative interest

### For Teaching

**SOURCE (SECONDARY):** "I lean into my vulnerability and humanness and happily let my students raise their status to become more engaged in their own learning. I admit when I don't know an answer or don't know how to spell something. I play up moments when I'm confused or forgetful, and this helps my students step up and help out." — Practitioner application

### For Personal Creativity

**SOURCE (SECONDARY):** "I tap into my stupid self before I start writing and my naughty self to be more fun at dinner parties." — Practitioner application

**SOURCE (PRIMARY):** "To truly be a great improviser you must enter into an open state of mind, you must listen and react to your teammates, you must take risks, suggest new ideas and actions and solve problems, all of which are valuable in everyday life." — Impro analysis

## 12. CONSOLIDATED FRAMEWORK

### Johnstone's Core Principles

1. **Spontaneity over Planning** - Don't plan ahead; respond in the moment
2. **Accept All Offers** - Say "Yes, And" to everything; never block
3. **Be Obvious** - Your first thought is the most original
4. **Status Awareness** - Every interaction involves status negotiation
5. **Reincorporate** - Look back, not forward, for story structure
6. **Interrupt Routines** - Disruption creates narrative
7. **Make Others Look Good** - "Don't do your best. Make other people look wonderful."
8. **Embrace Failure** - "Screw up and look happy"

### The Spontaneous Storytelling Process

1. **Establish a Platform** - Create a baseline reality/routine
2. **Accept Offers** - Whatever happens, accept and build on it
3. **Be Obvious** - Go with your first instinct
4. **Tilt** - Introduce disruption to the routine
5. **Explore the New Routine** - Follow where the disruption leads
6. **Reincorporate** - Bring back earlier elements
7. **Tilt Again** - Continue the pattern
8. **Complete the Circle** - End by resolving reincorporated elements

### Story Structure Template

```
PLATFORM (The Normal World)
    ↓
TILT (Something disrupts normality)
    ↓
NEW ROUTINE (Characters respond to disruption)
    ↓
TILT (Another disruption)
    ↓
REINCORPORATION (Earlier element returns)
    ↓
RESOLUTION (Circle completes)
```

### Status Dynamics Checklist

**To Raise Your Status:**
- Move less, especially your head
- Speak more slowly
- Take up more space
- Maintain eye contact
- Finish sentences completely
- Pause before responding

**To Lower Your Status:**
- Move more, fidget
- Speak faster
- Take up less space
- Break eye contact
- Trail off sentences
- Fill silence quickly

### Quality Criteria

**Excellent Spontaneous Stories:**
- Accept all offers
- Reincorporate earlier elements
- Status dynamics are clear and shifting
- The "obvious" choices feel fresh
- Interruptions feel natural
- Story feels complete (circles back)

**Weak Spontaneous Stories:**
- Blocking offers
- Random events without connection
- No status awareness
- Trying to be clever
- Events happen without disruption
- Story trails off without resolution

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Trying to be clever** - Leads to boring, common ideas
2. **Blocking offers** - Kills momentum and collaboration
3. **Planning ahead** - Prevents authentic spontaneity
4. **Fear of the obvious** - Your first thought is best
5. **Ignoring what came before** - Leads to meandering sequences
6. **Static status** - Makes interactions feel flat
7. **Avoiding failure** - Mistakes are opportunities
