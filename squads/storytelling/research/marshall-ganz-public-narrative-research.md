## Executive Summary

Marshall Ganz (born March 14, 1943) is the Rita E. Hauser Senior Lecturer in Leadership, Organizing, and Civil Society at Harvard Kennedy School. A legendary organizer who dropped out of Harvard to participate in Mississippi Freedom Summer (1964) and subsequently spent 16 years with Cesar Chavez and the United Farm Workers, Ganz developed the Public Narrative framework that powered Barack Obama's 2008 grassroots campaign success.

Public Narrative is a leadership practice that translates values into action through storytelling. It consists of three interconnected stories: Story of Self (why I am called), Story of Us (why we are called together), and Story of Now (why we must act immediately). Each story follows the Challenge-Choice-Outcome structure that makes narratives compelling and motivating.

Ganz's framework is grounded in the insight that **values inspire action through emotion**. Unlike analytical arguments that engage only the head, stories engage both head and heart, providing the moral and emotional resources needed to overcome fear, isolation, and inertia and instead act with hope, solidarity, and self-efficacy.

## Section 1: Biography and Background

### Origins and Early Life

Marshall Ganz was born March 14, 1943, and grew up in a Jewish household where narrative was fundamental. His father was a rabbi and his mother a teacher. This upbringing instilled in him a deep appreciation for the power of story in religious and cultural transmission.

**Source:** [Narrative Arts Interview](https://narrativearts.org/interview-with-marshall-ganz-on-public-narrative/)

### Civil Rights Movement and United Farm Workers

Introduced to organizing during the American civil rights movement, Ganz participated in Mississippi Freedom Summer in 1964. He then joined forces with Cesar Chavez and the United Farm Workers, working on their staff for sixteen years. During this time, he helped develop the organizing strategies that enabled the UFW to triumph over the California grape industry.

**Key Insight from UFW Experience:**
"In 1965, when some 800 Filipino grape workers began to strike, the UFW soon joined the action with 2,000 Mexican workers and turned the strike into a civil rights struggle. They engaged in civil disobedience, mobilized support from churches and students, boycotted growers, and transformed their struggle into La Causa."

**Source:** [Wikipedia - Marshall Ganz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Ganz)

### Strategic Capacity Concept

Ganz's UFW experience led him to formulate his concept of "strategic capacity" - explaining why Chavez's farmworker organizing succeeded while earlier efforts by other organizations failed.

**Definition:** Strategy is "how we turn what we have into what we need to get what we want."

**Three Elements of Strategic Capacity:**
1. **Motivation** - Greater commitment and engagement of leaders
2. **Access to Relevant Knowledge** - Deep understanding of community and culture
3. **Deliberation** - Open decision-making processes that generate fresh tactical ideas

**Source:** [Why David Sometimes Wins](https://www.amazon.com/Why-David-Sometimes-Wins-Organization/dp/0199757852)

### Obama Campaign and Camp Obama

In 2007, Ganz met with Barack Obama and David Axelrod at Harvard. He was subsequently charged with setting up the Camp Obama network - intensive community organizing-style training camps where volunteers learned to tell Obama's story and build a grassroots movement.

**Key Quote:** "The Camp Obamas were the core device we used. And that's where we offered – the first thing we taught people was narrative."

**Impact:** The success was phenomenal, generating one of the most effective and broad-based presidential campaigns in American history. While Axelrod was the numbers brain behind the strategy, Ganz was the brains behind the movement-building.

**Source:** [Nieman Reports](https://niemanreports.org/articles/marshall-ganz-narrative/)

### Harvard Kennedy School

Ganz returned to Harvard, earning his PhD in sociology in 2000. He has been teaching organizing at HKS for nearly 30 years and has trained more than 6,000 students in 35 countries. He is the founder of the Leading Change Network.

**Source:** [Harvard Kennedy School](https://www.hks.harvard.edu/educational-programs/executive-education/public-narrative-leadership-storytelling-and-action)

## Section 2: The Public Narrative Framework

### Definition and Purpose

**Definition:** Public narrative is a leadership practice of translating values into action through storytelling. It is not primarily a form of self-expression, but an exercise of leadership aimed at motivating others to join in action on behalf of a shared purpose.

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

### The Three Questions of Hillel

Ganz grounds his framework in the ancient wisdom of Rabbi Hillel the Elder:
- "If I am not for myself, who will be?" → Story of Self
- "If I am for myself alone, what am I?" → Story of Us
- "If not now, when?" → Story of Now

**Source:** [Global Academy Media Transcript](https://www.globalacademy.media/transcript-marshall-ganz-the-power-of-storytelling/)

### The Three Stories

**Story of Self:** Communicates the values that have called you to leadership. It reveals "why you were called to what you have been called to" through sharing personal experiences that demonstrate your values and commitment.

**Story of Us:** Communicates the values shared by those in action. It creates collective identity by highlighting shared struggles, aspirations, and experiences that bind a community together.

**Story of Now:** Communicates an urgent challenge to those values that demands action now. It transforms the present moment into one of challenge, hope, and choice.

**Source:** [Narrative Arts](https://narrativearts.org/article/public-narrative/)

### Integration of the Three Stories

The three stories work together: "You had to have a story to explain why. You had to have a strategy to address how. And you had to have a structure to make clear how we were organizing ourselves to actually implement strategy and enact the story."

**Source:** [Nieman Reports](https://niemanreports.org/articles/marshall-ganz-narrative/)

## Section 3: Challenge-Choice-Outcome Structure

### The Core Story Architecture

Every story in public narrative follows the Challenge-Choice-Outcome structure. This is what makes stories compelling and memorable.

**Challenge:** A plot begins with an unexpected challenge that confronts a character with an urgent need to pay attention, to make a choice for which they are unprepared. Listeners lean in when presented with tension, uncertainty, or the unknown.

**Choice:** The character makes a specific decision about how to respond to the challenge. This is where values become visible - our moral choices reveal who we are.

**Outcome:** The results of the choice, including what was learned. The outcome teaches a lesson that is felt emotionally, not just understood conceptually.

**Source:** [Bill Moyers](https://billmoyers.com/content/how-to-tell-your-story-of-self/)

### Key Questions for Developing Challenge-Choice-Outcome

**For Challenge:**
- Why did you feel it was a challenge?
- What was so challenging about it?
- Why was it YOUR challenge?

**For Choice:**
- Why did you make the choice you did?
- Where did you get the courage (or not)?
- Where did you get the hope (or not)?

**For Outcome:**
- How did the outcome feel?
- Why did it feel that way?
- What did it teach you?

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

### Why This Structure Works

"A story structure is made up of three elements: plot, character, and moral, but a story comes alive when the character faces a challenge, makes a choice, and experiences the outcome."

The structure works because:
1. Stories capture our interest when the character meets unexpected challenge
2. Decisions convey values and provoke emotional resonance
3. The moral becomes a teaching of the heart, not just the head

**Source:** [The Commons Library](https://commonslibrary.org/the-power-of-story-the-story-of-self-us-and-now/)

## Section 4: Story of Self - Deep Dive

### Purpose and Definition

A Story of Self explains "why you were called to what you have been called to." Everyone has a story about an experience that got them involved in a given cause. The purpose is to create common ground with your audience by sharing a story that reflects the values that brought you to work on your given issue.

**Key Point:** "When you do public work, you have a responsibility to offer a public account of who you are, why you do what you do, and where you hope to lead. If you don't author your public story, others will, and they may not tell it in the way that you like."

**Source:** [Bill Moyers](https://billmoyers.com/content/how-to-tell-your-story-of-self/)

### Choice Points as Foundation

We construct stories of self around **choice points** - moments when we faced a challenge, made a choice, experienced an outcome, and learned a moral. These are moments in our lives when values are formed because of a need to choose in the face of great uncertainty.

**Source:** [ACLU Ohio Story of Self Workbook](https://www.acluohio.org/app/uploads/2021/04/StoryOfSelfWorkbook_2020-1209.pdf)

### Sources of Your Story

**Family and Childhood:**
- Your parents and family
- Experiences growing up
- The community in which you grew up
- Your role models
- Your school

**Life Choices:**
- Schools you went to
- The career you chose
- Your partner and family
- Your hobbies, interests and talents
- Challenges you've overcome

**Key Questions:**
- When did you first care about being heard, or learn that you were concerned about the issue on which you want to take action?
- When did you feel you had to do something about it?
- Why did you feel you could?
- What were the circumstances?
- What specific choice did you make?

**Source:** [Leading Change Network Worksheet](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/public-narrative-worksheet-worksheet-to-formulate-your-story-of-self/)

### Practical Guidelines for Story of Self

1. **Time Constraint:** Learn to tell a two-minute story as the first step in mastering public narrative. The time limit focuses on getting to the point, offering images rather than lots of words.

2. **Focus on ONE Key Event:** Focus on one key event, relationship, or place rather than attempting comprehensive autobiography.

3. **Show, Don't Tell:** Express values as lived experience, not abstract principles.

4. **Iterative Process:** Public narrative requires learning a process, not writing a script. It can be learned only by telling, listening, reflecting, and telling again.

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

## Section 5: Story of Us - Deep Dive

### Purpose and Definition

A Story of Us communicates values shared by those whom you hope to motivate to act. It highlights the shared values, struggles, and aspirations of the community or group, creating a sense of collective identity and reinforcing the idea that individuals are part of something larger than themselves.

**Key Insight:** "A story of 'us' not only articulates the values of our community; it can also distinguish our community from another, thus reducing uncertainty about what to expect from those with whom we interact."

**Source:** [Narrative Arts](https://narrativearts.org/article/public-narrative/)

### Building Collective Identity

"We participate in many us's: family, community, faith, organization, profession, nation, or movement. A story of us expresses the values, the experiences, shared by the us we hope to evoke at the time."

The goal is to create a sense of unity, togetherness, and focus on the shared values of your listeners.

**Source:** [The Commons Library](https://commonslibrary.org/the-power-of-story-the-story-of-self-us-and-now/)

### Structure of Story of Us

Just like any good story, stories of us recount moments when the community experienced a challenge, choice, and outcome expressive of shared values. These may be:
- Founding moments
- Moments of crisis
- Moments of triumph
- Disaster
- Resilience
- Humor

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

### Key Questions for Story of Us

- What values do you share with this community?
- What experiences have had the greatest impact on this community?
- What challenges has it faced?
- What change does this community hope for and why?

**Source:** [The Commons Library](https://commonslibrary.org/the-power-of-story-the-story-of-self-us-and-now/)

### The Challenge of Inclusive Stories

"Religions, races, ethnicities, and nations tell such stories brilliantly but often do it in a way that excludes—and makes enemies of—those outside the magic circle. The challenge for the 21st century leader is to tell a story of us that includes people of all backgrounds who are fighting for the same cause."

**Source:** [Sojourners - Why Stories Matter](https://sojo.net/magazine/march-2009/why-stories-matter)

### From Individual to Collective

Often after we've heard others' Stories of Self and started building relationships together, we discover that we face similar challenges rooted in deep systems of power inequality. A good Story of Us doesn't just convey the root of our challenges, but also lifts up our heroes and stories of even small successes.

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

## Section 6: Story of Now - Deep Dive

### Purpose and Definition

A Story of Now communicates the urgent challenge to values that demands action NOW. It transforms the present into a moment of challenge, hope, and choice.

**Key Quote:** "A 'story of now' is urgent, an urgency based on threat, or, equally, on opportunity; it is meant to inspire others to drop other things and pay attention; it is rooted in the values you celebrate in your story of self and us but poses a challenge to those values."

**Source:** [Narrative Arts](https://narrativearts.org/article/public-narrative/)

### The Fierce Urgency of Now

The Story of Now represents "the fierce urgency of now." It realizes, after the sharing of values and aspirations, that the world is not as it ought to be. Instead, it is as it is. And that is a challenge to us.

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

### Components of Story of Now

A Story of Now must include:
1. **The urgent challenge** this community now faces
2. **The choices** it must make
3. **The hope** to which "we" can aspire
4. **A concrete first step** people can take immediately

**Source:** [Narrative Arts](https://narrativearts.org/article/public-narrative/)

### Balancing Urgency and Hope

The challenge is to create urgency that gets engagement while inspiring hope to counter fear, empathy to counter isolation, and self-efficacy to counter self-doubt.

**Emotional Balance:**
- Urgency and anger → Counter inertia and apathy
- Hope, solidarity, YCMAD ("You Can Make A Difference") → Counter fear, isolation, self-doubt

**Source:** [Commons Library - Marshall Ganz Quotes](https://commonslibrary.org/marshall-ganz-quotes-and-wisdom/)

### Key Questions for Story of Now

- What's the urgent challenge?
- What's your vision of success?
- What choice are you calling your community to make?
- How can they act together beginning immediately?

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

## Section 7: Values, Emotions, and Motivation

### The Core Insight

**"The key to motivation is understanding that values inspire action through emotion."**

This is the foundational insight of public narrative. We experience our values emotionally - they are what actually move us to act. Because stories allow us to express our values not as abstract principles, but as lived experience, they have the power to move others to action.

**Source:** [Bill Moyers](https://billmoyers.com/content/how-to-tell-your-story-of-self/)

### Why Emotions Matter

"A story communicates fear, hope, and anxiety, and because we feel it, we get the moral not just as a concept, but as a teaching of our hearts. That's the power of story."

Narrative is not talking "about" values; rather narrative embodies and communicates values. The moral of a successful story is emotionally experienced understanding, not only conceptual understanding.

**Source:** [Sojourners - Why Stories Matter](https://sojo.net/magazine/march-2009/why-stories-matter)

### Emotions That Inhibit Action

- **Inertia** - Lack of urgency to change
- **Fear** - Paralysis in face of threat
- **Self-doubt** - Belief that one cannot make a difference
- **Isolation** - Feeling alone in the struggle
- **Apathy** - Not caring about outcomes

### Emotions That Enable Action

- **Urgency** - Recognition that action is needed now
- **Hope** - Belief that change is possible
- **Anger** - Righteous response to injustice
- **Solidarity** - Sense of being part of something larger
- **YCMAD** - "You Can Make A Difference" - self-efficacy
- **Self-worth** - Dignity and value in the struggle

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

### The Head, Heart, and Hands

Practicing leadership requires engaging:
1. **The Heart** - Motivation (through narrative)
2. **The Head** - Strategy (through analysis)
3. **The Hands** - Action (through organizing)

"Because it engages the 'head' and the 'heart,' narrative can instruct and inspire – teaching us not only why we should act, but moving us to act."

**Source:** [Global Academy Media](https://www.globalacademy.media/transcript-marshall-ganz-the-power-of-storytelling/)

## Section 8: The Five Leadership Practices of Organizing

### Overview

Ganz deconstructs organizing into five interconnected leadership practices. These practices build the capacity for collective action.

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/our-approach-to-change/)

### Practice 1: Public Narrative (Storytelling)

**Purpose:** Build shared values through story
**How:** Share personal origin stories to connect values with others; tell stories of self, us, and now to motivate action

### Practice 2: Relationship Building

**Purpose:** Develop shared commitments
**How:** Create value-based, transformational (not transactional) relationships
**Key Insight:** "The importance of relationships, rather than campaign platforms, dominated the Camp Obama training program."

### Practice 3: Structure (Team Building)

**Purpose:** Create spaces for collaboration
**How:** Distribute leadership through "snowflake" models with interdependent teams
**Key Concept:** In Obama campaign, they remodeled the volunteer structure so multiple leaders shared responsibility rather than relying on single precinct captains.

### Practice 4: Strategy

**Purpose:** Develop theory of change
**How:** Collective strategy development rather than consultant-imposed plans; turn resources into power
**Definition:** "Strategy is how we turn what we have into what we need to get what we want."

### Practice 5: Action

**Purpose:** Execute measurable campaigns
**How:** Generate both societal wins and individual/organizational growth; enable continuous learning

**Source:** [Leading Change Network - What is Organizing](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-organizing/)

### How Practices Work Together

"Though organizing is not a linear process, organizers use the first three practices (stories, relationships, structure) to build power within a community, while the last two practices (strategy, action) are about wielding that power in order to create change."

**Source:** [Commons Library - What is Organizing](https://commonslibrary.org/what-is-organizing-an-introduction-based-on-the-work-of-marshall-ganz/)

## Section 9: Practical Workshop Methodology

### The Learning Process

"Organizing is a practice – a way of doing things. It's like learning to ride a bike. No matter how many books you read about bike riding, they are of little use when it comes to getting on the bike. And when you get on, the first thing that will happen is that you will fall."

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/public-narrative-online-course-by-marshall-ganz-videos/)

### Workshop Structure (Harvard Kennedy School Program)

1. **Live lectures** by Professor Marshall Ganz
2. **Peer-learning sessions** led by skilled facilitators
3. **One-on-one coaching sessions** with public narrative experts

In the first seven sessions, participants learn to craft their own public narrative and coach others. They then learn how to use public narrative to create an empathetic bridge enabling others to respond to urgent challenges.

**Source:** [Harvard Kennedy School](https://www.hks.harvard.edu/educational-programs/executive-education/public-narrative-leadership-storytelling-and-action)

### Crafting Exercise Process

1. **Compose:** Spend time writing your own story
2. **Share:** Tell your story to others in small groups
3. **Feedback:** Receive questions meant to deepen the challenge, choice, and outcome
4. **Revise:** Incorporate feedback and tell again

**Key Questions for Feedback:**
- What made your challenge a challenge?
- Where did you get the strength to make the choice you did?
- What details made the story vivid?

**Source:** [Bill Moyers](https://billmoyers.com/content/how-to-tell-your-story-of-self/)

### Time Constraints and Focus

"We can never tell our whole life story in two minutes. We are learning to tell a two-minute story as the first step in mastering the craft of public narrative. The time limit focuses on getting to the point, offering images rather than lots of words, and choosing choice points strategically."

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/public-narrative-online-course-by-marshall-ganz-videos/)

### The Iterative Nature

"This is an iterative – and non-linear – process. Each time you tell your story, you will adapt it – to make yourself clearer, to adjust to a different audience, to locate yourself in a different context."

"Public narrative requires learning a process, not writing a script. It can be learned only by telling, listening, reflecting, and telling again – over, over and over."

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-public-narrative-self-us-and-now-public-narrative-worksheet-working-paper/)

## Section 10: Application Examples and Case Studies

### Obama Campaign 2008

**Context:** The most successful application of public narrative in political organizing.

**Implementation:**
- "Camp Obama" training sessions across the country
- Volunteers learned to tell their own stories before learning campaign talking points
- Focus on relationships rather than platform positions
- Snowflake structure distributing leadership

**Results:** One of the most effective and broad-based presidential campaigns in American history, with unprecedented grassroots participation.

**Key Quote:** "Participants discovered their existing capacities rather than acquiring external expertise, transforming how they engaged voters and built movements."

**Source:** [Narrative Arts Interview](https://narrativearts.org/interview-with-marshall-ganz-on-public-narrative/)

### United Farm Workers

**Context:** The original laboratory for Ganz's organizing principles.

**Implementation:**
- Turned labor strike into civil rights struggle (La Causa)
- Combined civil disobedience with church and student mobilization
- Boycotts as strategic action

**Results:** Triumph over California grape industry after years of struggle.

**Key Insight:** Success came from strategic capacity - greater motivation, deeper community knowledge, and open deliberation processes.

**Source:** [Wikipedia - Marshall Ganz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Ganz)

### Global Applications

The framework has enabled campaigns addressing:
- Child poverty (Kenya)
- Reproductive health stigma (India)
- Child marriage prevention (Lebanon)
- Incarceration costs (Florida)
- Environmental protection (Serbia)

**Source:** [Leading Change Network](https://leadingchangenetwork.org/resource_center/what-is-organizing/)

### Movement Building Distinction

**Critical Warning from Ganz:** Public narrative should not be used as mere marketing. Movement-building differs fundamentally from selling products—it's about enabling people to recognize and act on their own agency through authentic engagement.

**Source:** [Narrative Arts Interview](https://narrativearts.org/interview-with-marshall-ganz-on-public-narrative/)

## Consolidated Framework: The Ganz Public Narrative Method

### Core Principles

1. **Values Inspire Action Through Emotion**
   - Stories embody values as lived experience
   - Emotional understanding moves people to act
   - Head AND heart must be engaged

2. **Narrative is Leadership Practice**
   - Stories enable others to achieve shared purpose
   - Leaders must author their public story
   - Narrative builds collective identity and motivates action

3. **Challenge-Choice-Outcome is Universal Structure**
   - All compelling stories follow this arc
   - Choice points reveal values
   - Outcomes teach lessons of the heart

4. **Self-Us-Now is Complete Narrative Arc**
   - Self establishes personal calling and credibility
   - Us builds collective identity and shared values
   - Now creates urgency and calls to specific action

5. **Learning Through Practice, Not Script**
   - Narrative is iterative process
   - Tell, listen, reflect, tell again
   - Adapt to audience and context

**Source:** Multiple Ganz materials consolidated

### Complete Process

**Step 1: Identify Your Choice Point (Self)**
- Reflect on moments when values were formed
- Find the challenge that called you to action
- Articulate the choice you made and why
- Share the outcome and what it taught you

**Step 2: Connect to Community (Us)**
- Identify shared values with your audience
- Find community choice points (founding, crisis, triumph)
- Build inclusive identity that welcomes all
- Lift up heroes and small victories

**Step 3: Create Urgency (Now)**
- Name the current challenge to shared values
- Present the choice the community faces
- Inspire hope while acknowledging threat
- Offer concrete first step for immediate action

**Step 4: Weave Together**
- Flow naturally from self to us to now
- Let personal calling connect to community purpose
- Make urgency specific and actionable
- End with clear invitation to join

**Source:** Synthesis of Ganz methodology

### Structure Template

```
STORY OF SELF (2 minutes)
├── Challenge: What was the moment that called you?
├── Choice: What did you decide to do? Why?
└── Outcome: What happened? What did you learn?

STORY OF US (2 minutes)
├── Challenge: What challenge does our community face?
├── Choice: How have we responded in the past?
└── Outcome: What have we achieved together?

STORY OF NOW (2 minutes)
├── Challenge: What urgent threat/opportunity faces us?
├── Choice: What must we decide NOW?
├── Hope: What is possible if we act?
└── Action: What is the first concrete step?
```

### Quality Criteria

**Excellent Narrative:**
- Uses specific details and images
- Reveals values through action, not abstraction
- Creates emotional connection with audience
- Builds to clear call to action
- Feels authentic and personal

**Weak Narrative:**
- Abstract principles without lived experience
- Comprehensive biography instead of focused story
- Appeals only to logic, not emotion
- Vague or missing call to action
- Feels like marketing, not movement

**Source:** Synthesis from Ganz teaching materials

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Telling your whole life story** - Focus on ONE choice point
2. **Abstract value statements** - Show values through action
3. **Exclusive "us" that creates enemies** - Build inclusive identity
4. **Urgency without hope** - Balance threat with possibility
5. **Hope without action** - Always include concrete first step
6. **Marketing disguised as movement** - Enable agency, don't sell

**Source:** Synthesis from multiple Ganz sources
