## Executive Summary

Shawn Coyne is a veteran book publishing professional with over 30 years of experience who created the Story Grid methodology - a comprehensive system for analyzing, diagnosing, and fixing story problems. The methodology provides writers with concrete tools to understand why stories work or fail, derived from Coyne's extensive experience editing hundreds of books including bestsellers across all genres.

The Story Grid is fundamentally an editorial diagnostic tool that treats story analysis like a "CT Scan" - revealing the structural health of a narrative by examining its component parts. The methodology integrates insights from Aristotle's Poetics, Gustav Freytag's dramatic structure, Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey, Robert McKee's "Story," and contemporary cognitive science into a unified, practical framework.

**Key Components:**
- The Five Commandments of Storytelling (structural requirements for every story unit)
- Genre Five-Leaf Clover (classification system for story expectations)
- Four Core Framework (needs, values, emotions, events)
- Obligatory Scenes and Conventions (genre-specific requirements)
- The Foolscap Global Story Grid (one-page story outline)
- The Story Grid Spreadsheet (scene-by-scene diagnostic tool)

## 2. THE FIVE COMMANDMENTS OF STORYTELLING

### Overview

The Five Commandments are the structural requirements that must appear in every unit of story - from individual beats to the global story level. They represent the fundamental pattern of change that makes stories work.

> "Every effective story has five structural components that work together to communicate a controlling idea in a way that bypasses readers' critical minds to touch their hearts and change their worldviews." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### The Five Commandments Detailed

#### 1. INCITING INCIDENT

**Definition:** An event or disturbance that sets the scene into action by destabilizing the protagonist's life.

**Function:** The inciting incident throws the protagonist's life out of balance. It incites action. Without it, nothing happens.

**Two Types:**
- **Causal:** The protagonist makes an active choice
- **Coincidental:** Something unexpected, random, or accidental happens

**Critical Rule:** The inciting incident must promise the climactic action. The inciting incident does not tie to the climax = major structural flaw.

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Five Commandments article)

#### 2. PROGRESSIVE COMPLICATIONS

**Definition:** The problems the protagonist faces that get progressively more difficult as the story advances.

**Function:** Forces the character deeper into the story and further away from where they were before the inciting incident.

**Quantity:** Sometimes a scene has one complication, sometimes many. The key is they must become progressively more complicated.

**The Turning Point (Critical Sub-Component):**
The turning point is actually a progressive complication that gives rise to the crisis. It's the moment when the value shifts.

**Turning Point Types:**
- **Action:** A character takes an action or an event takes place (e.g., a bomb blows up)
- **Revelation:** New information enters the story

> "If you are turning your scenes the same way over and over again, it would be good to know that right? Repetitious turns will turn off a reader or viewer subconsciously." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Five Commandments articles)

#### 3. CRISIS

**Definition:** A question that the protagonist must answer - a dilemma with meaningful consequences.

**Function:** Forces the protagonist to make an active choice rather than remaining passive.

**Critical Rule:** If there aren't consequences, or the decision is a no-brainer, it's not a crisis. Something must be at stake.

**Crisis Types:**
- **Best Bad Choice:** Choosing between two horrible things
- **Irreconcilable Goods:** Choosing between two wonderful things

> "The crisis poses a real choice between incompatible options with meaningful stakes. It is always a binary 'this or that' choice." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Five Commandments article)

#### 4. CLIMAX

**Definition:** The active answer to the question raised by the crisis.

**Function:** The culmination of the scene's tension and conflict, the moment of highest intensity where the protagonist's decision plays out.

**Critical Rule:** The climax has actual consequences depending on what the protagonist chose in the crisis. The climax must mirror the inciting incident to show how the protagonist has changed.

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Five Commandments article)

#### 5. RESOLUTION

**Definition:** What happens as a result of the protagonist's choice during the climax.

**Function:** Signifies the change that occurred; the new status quo.

**Critical Rule:** If there's no change in value, the scene doesn't work. If you can't identify the turning point, the value shift is probably not strong enough.

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Five Commandments article)

### Application Across Story Units

The Five Commandments appear in EVERY unit of story:
- Beat
- Scene
- Sequence
- Act/Quadrant
- Subplot
- Global Story

> "The five commandments are in each unit of story. The units of story are the beat, scene, sequence, act, subplot and global story." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

## 3. STORY STRUCTURE: BEGINNING HOOK, MIDDLE BUILD, ENDING PAYOFF

### Three-Act Framework

The Story Grid organizes stories into three main movements:

| Act | Name | Percentage | Purpose |
|-----|------|------------|---------|
| Act 1 | Beginning Hook | 25% | Hook the reader, establish genre and expectations |
| Act 2 | Middle Build | 50% | Build complications, raise stakes, deepen conflict |
| Act 3 | Ending Payoff | 25% | Pay off promises, provide catharsis |

### Scene Distribution

A novel should have 50-60 scenes:
- **Beginning Hook:** 12-15 scenes (25%)
- **Middle Build:** 25-30 scenes (50%)
- **Ending Payoff:** 12-15 scenes (25%)

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - How Many Scenes article)

### Beginning Hook

> "The BEGINNING is all about HOOKING your reader...getting them so deeply curious and involved in the Story that there is no way they'll abandon it until they know how it turns out." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Purpose:**
- Let the reader know what kind of story they're going to be told
- Establish the genre
- Set up objects of desire
- Establish things that will be paid off later

### Middle Build

> "The Middle is about BUILDING progressive complications that bring the stress and pressure down so hard on your lead character/s that they are forced to take huge risks so that they can return to 'normal.'" (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Purpose:**
- Up the ante
- Raise the stakes
- Progressively complicate the situation
- Build conflict and tension
- Bring the hero to their lowest point

### Ending Payoff

> "In Act III (the end), all the things that you set up get paid off. Either the hero gets what she wants and needs, or she doesn't, and that outcome has to happen in a way that is both surprising and inevitable." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Purpose:**
- Repay the reader for investing their time
- Fulfill promises made in the Beginning Hook
- Provide emotional catharsis

## 4. THE GENRE FIVE-LEAF CLOVER

### Overview

The Story Grid Genre Five-Leaf Clover is the tool used to define genres. Each leaf helps determine a different element of the story experience.

### The Five Leaves

#### 1. TIME GENRE

**Definition:** How the reader experiences the time it takes to consume the story.

**Categories:**
- Short Story
- Novella
- Novel
- Series

#### 2. STRUCTURE GENRE

**Definition:** How the story's architecture is organized.

**Categories:**
- Archplot (classical design)
- Miniplot (minimalist design)
- Antiplot (anti-structure)

#### 3. REALITY GENRE

**Definition:** How much readers must suspend disbelief when building the story world.

**Categories:**
- Factualism (real world, true events)
- Realism (real world, fictional events)
- Fantasy (alternate world with different rules)
- Absurdism (reality that defies logical explanation)

**Important Note:** Science fiction and fantasy are reality choices, not content choices. They're about building an alternative universe, but the storytelling is really about content.

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Genre articles)

#### 4. STYLE GENRE

**Definition:** How emotions are handled, what form the story takes, and what language is used.

**Broad Categories:**
- Drama
- Comedy

**Mediums:**
- Documentary
- Musical
- Dance
- Literary
- Theatrical
- Cinematic
- Epistolary
- Animation

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Style Genre article)

#### 5. CONTENT GENRE (Most Important)

**Definition:** What is contained in the story - determines the need and value at stake.

> "The Content leaf is the most useful in terms of constructing a story." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**The 12 Content Genres:**

| Genre | Core Value | Core Emotion | Core Need |
|-------|------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Action** | Life/Death | Excitement | Survival |
| **Horror** | Life/Death (Self-preservation) | Terror | Safety |
| **Thriller** | Life/Death (Human Damnation) | Dread/Anxiety | Security |
| **Crime** | Justice/Injustice | Intrigue | Fair Play |
| **Western** | Freedom/Oppression | Nostalgia/Pride | Freedom |
| **War** | Honor/Dishonor | Respect/Fear | Survival of Group |
| **Love** | Love/Hate | Romance | Connection |
| **Performance** | Respect/Shame | Admiration | Self-Actualization |
| **Society** | Power/Impotence | Concern | Belonging |
| **Status** | Success/Failure | Admiration/Pity | Esteem |
| **Worldview** | Sophistication/Naivete | Transcendence | Knowledge |
| **Morality** | Altruism/Selfishness | Satisfaction/Contempt | Self-Transcendence |

(PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid - Content Genre article)

## 5. THE FOUR CORE FRAMEWORK

### Overview

The Four Core Framework identifies the essential elements that spark emotional connection and catharsis:

> "At Story Grid, great stories are built of four essential elements: Core Needs, Core Life Values, Core Emotions, and Core Events." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### The Four Elements

#### 1. CORE NEED

**Definition:** The subconscious object of desire; the Truth the protagonist needs to learn.

**Function:** When avatars fulfill (or fail to fulfill) their Core Need, it provides catharsis for the reader.

**Source:** Based on Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs - universal needs shared by all humans.

#### 2. CORE VALUE

**Definition:** The yardstick measuring where the avatar's actions are along a spectrum specific to the story.

**Function:** Dictates the genre of the story.

**Examples:**
- Action story: Death → Life spectrum
- Crime story: Injustice → Justice spectrum
- Love story: Hate → Love spectrum

**The Value Spectrum (Core Four):**
1. **Positive Value** (e.g., Life, Justice, Love)
2. **Contrary Value** (opposite but not extreme)
3. **Negative Value** (e.g., Death, Injustice, Hate)
4. **Negation of the Negation** (fate worse than the negative)

#### 3. CORE EMOTION

**Definition:** What we want the reader to feel as they experience the story.

**Function:** The particular "flavor" of catharsis generated when the core value shifts.

> "If we are not causing rises and falls on a single core emotion—fear, intrigue, esteem, romance, triumph, etc.—the story will fail to engage the reader and they will put our book down." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

#### 4. CORE EVENT

**Definition:** The scene where the story reaches the height of emotional tension - the obligatory scene of the genre.

**Function:** Answers the question raised by the inciting incident; moment of emotional catharsis.

**Rule:** The Core Need and Value are always in peril in the Core Event.

### The Framework Formula

> "A core need satisfied or denied through the change of a core value in a core event that elicits a core emotion." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

## 6. OBLIGATORY SCENES AND CONVENTIONS

### Definitions

**Conventions:** Elements in a story that set up a change in the protagonist; the setup that creates expectations.

**Obligatory Scenes/Moments:** The must-have events, revelations, or decisions that pay off the raised expectations of the conventions.

> "Coyne compares conventions and obligatory scenes to 'the legs, backrests, and seats of chairs. Without them, you don't have a chair. But if your chairs are just like everyone else's, no one will want to sit on your furniture.'" (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### Why They Matter

> "Deciding what Genre(s) your Story will inhabit will tell you exactly what you need to do to satisfy your potential audience's expectations. You must know what your reader is expecting before you can possibly satisfy her." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Shawn Coyne)

### Genre-Specific Examples

#### Thriller Genre

**Conventions:**
- A crime must occur early, revealing a clue about the villain's MacGuffin
- Speech in praise of the villain
- MacGuffin (the thing everyone is after)

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Hero at the mercy of the villain scene (protagonist seemingly incapable of escape)
- False ending (villain reasserts one final time)

#### Love Genre

**Conventions:**
- Lovers must have some obstacle keeping them apart
- Triangle (external force that tempts one or both lovers)

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Lovers meet
- Confession of love scene
- First kiss scene
- Lovers break up
- Proof of love
- Lovers reunite

### Innovation vs. Obligation

> "Coyne warns that some writers 'love Genre because they think they can just recycle old scenes from the Genre's vault to fulfill these obligations. But if you rehash something you saw on a Mannix episode from the 1970s, you will sorely disappoint your reader.'" (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

> "Ditching the Conventions and Obligatory Moments 'cannot ever be an option. Your reader matters too much. If you cheap out on her, she'll never trust you with a story again.'" (PRIMARY SOURCE: Shawn Coyne)

## 7. THE FOOLSCAP GLOBAL STORY GRID

### Origin

The Foolscap method was inspired by Steven Pressfield and his mentor Norm Stahl, a prolific documentary filmmaker who needed a macro one-page outline for entire projects.

> "He told Pressfield to figure out his beginning, middle, and end of his story, put it on one sheet of paper, and then write the damn thing." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### Purpose

The Foolscap is a one-page document containing key elements of the global story:

- Quick reference for making story decisions
- Ensures every unit contributes to compelling narrative
- 30,000 foot view of the story
- Reviewed at the beginning of each work session

### Structure

The Foolscap breaks down into four sections:

1. **Global Story Section:** Genre, expectations, external/internal content
2. **Beginning Hook:** Inciting incident, turning points, crisis
3. **Middle Build:** Progressive complications, all-is-lost moment, point of no return
4. **Ending Payoff:** Core event, climax, resolution

### Each Section Includes Five Commandments

For each movement (Beginning Hook, Middle Build, Ending Payoff):
- Inciting Incident
- Turning Point Progressive Complication
- Crisis
- Climax
- Resolution

### Writer vs. Editor Approach

> "Steve Pressfield's foolscap method is from the point of view of the writer before embarking on a first draft—because that's so intimidating, Steve keeps it very short. The difference is that Shawn Coyne's method is the editorial method." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

## 8. THE STORY GRID SPREADSHEET

### Purpose

The Story Grid Spreadsheet is the primary diagnostic tool for scene-by-scene analysis:

> "The spreadsheet is about making the puzzle pieces for the ailments of the story—it's like the vital signs of the story if you want to go with the metaphor of physical health. The Story Grid spreadsheet shows you what ails the story and when and where you need to fix it." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### Key Columns

| Column Group | Elements Tracked |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Identity** | Scene number, word count, chapter |
| **Summary** | One-sentence story event |
| **Change** | Value shift, polarity shift, turning point type |
| **Five Commandments** | II, PC/TP, Crisis, Climax, Resolution |
| **Continuity** | POV, time, duration, location |
| **Characters** | On-stage, off-stage |

### Value Shift Analysis

**Definition:** The movement between positive and negative values along a global spectrum.

**Tracking:**
- Value at beginning of scene (e.g., "safe")
- Value at end of scene (e.g., "threatened")
- Polarity shift (+ to - or - to +)
- Turning point moment

> "If there's no change in value, the scene doesn't work. If you can't identify the turning point, then the value shift is probably not strong enough." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### Diagnostic Process

> "If you start fixing before you have a complete diagnosis of all of your problems, you'll inevitably drive yourself crazy. Bailing out water from your leaky boat before you discover where the big hole is just burns precious time and energy. Inspect every part of the boat first to find out where the big and small holes are. Fix the big ones, then work your way down." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Analysis Order:**
1. Global-level problems
2. Sequence/quadrant problems
3. Scene-level problems

### Variety Check

The spreadsheet reveals patterns:
- Are turning points repetitive (all action or all revelation)?
- Is polarity shifting in varied directions?
- Are scenes working (values actually changing)?

## 9. INTERNAL GENRES: WORLDVIEW AND MORALITY

### Worldview Genre

**Core Value:** Sophistication ↔ Naivete
**Core Emotion:** Transcendence
**Core Need:** Knowledge/Understanding

**Subgenres:**

| Subgenre | Shift | Example |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **Maturation** | Naivete → Sophistication | To Kill a Mockingbird |
| **Education** | Meaninglessness → Meaning | Educating Rita |
| **Disillusionment** | Belief → Disillusionment | The Great Gatsby |
| **Revelation** | Ignorance → Wisdom | Oedipus Rex |

**Maturation Story Requirements:**
> "With the help of a mentor, the protagonist faces challenges to their flawed, black-and-white view of the world and comes to accept the world as it is, multi-layered and imperfect, to embrace better suited goals and actions." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Controlling Idea (Prescriptive):**
> "We gain new wisdom when we share our gifts with a multicolored and imperfect world that's neither all or nothing." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### Morality Genre

**Core Value:** Altruism ↔ Selfishness
**Core Emotion:** Satisfaction/Contempt
**Core Need:** Self-Transcendence

**Subgenres:**

| Subgenre | Arc | Example |
|----------|-----|---------|
| **Testing** | Strong → Tested → Remains steadfast or surrenders | Cool Hand Luke |
| **Punitive** | Selfish → More selfish → Deserved punishment | Wall Street |
| **Redemption** | Selfish → Challenged → Altruistic | A Christmas Carol |

**Redemption Story Requirements:**
> "Morality-Redemption stories must begin in the realm of SELFISHNESS and end with ALTRUISM, or it isn't a Redemption story." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Controlling Idea (Prescriptive):**
> "We transcend our own selfishness when we share our gifts for the benefit of others." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

## 10. STORY UNITS HIERARCHY

### The Hierarchy

```
Global Story
    ↓
Acts/Quadrants (4)
    ↓
Sequences (2-6 scenes each)
    ↓
Scenes
    ↓
Beats
```

### Unit Definitions

**Beat:** The foundational level; individual moments of exchange.

**Scene:** The building block of stories; dramatizes definable value shifts.

**Sequence:** A continuous series of 2-6 connected scenes tracking mid-level changes.

> "With the sequence unit of story, the gap is bridged between the larger frame-changing shifts at the level of the quadrant and the smaller value shifts at the level of the scene." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Quadrant/Act:** Demonstrates stages of development through the protagonist's ability to adapt to change.

> "Each of the four quadrants of a story demonstrates the protagonist's perception or approach to the story's primary conflict." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

**Global Story:** Contains all other units; has its own Five Commandments.

### Key Principle

> "Stories are about change. All stories are about change. The state of the world inside a story and inside each unit of a story is different at the end than it was at the beginning. In fact, the first thing to check when assessing any unit of a story is whether a life-altering change has taken place in the protagonist or another character." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

## 11. DIAGNOSTIC METHODOLOGY: FIXING STORY PROBLEMS

### The Story Grid as CT Scan

> "The Story Grid is a tool developed by editor Shawn Coyne to analyze stories and provide helpful editorial comments. It's like a CT Scan that takes a photo of the global story and tells the editor or writer what is working, what is not." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### What Story Grid Does

1. Tells a writer if a story "works" or "doesn't work"
2. Pinpoints story problems without emotionally abusing the writer
3. Reveals exactly where the Story has failed
4. Specifies the work necessary to fix problems
5. Provides tools to resuscitate "irredeemable" manuscripts

### Common Story Problems

| Problem | Diagnostic Sign | Solution |
|---------|-----------------|----------|
| No value shift | Scene feels flat | Identify and strengthen turning point |
| Missing inciting incident | Nothing "launches" the story | Add destabilizing event |
| Weak crisis | Character is passive | Create true dilemma with stakes |
| Repetitive turning points | Reader loses engagement | Vary between action and revelation |
| Missing obligatory scenes | Genre expectations unmet | Identify and add required scenes |
| Inciting incident doesn't match climax | Story feels disconnected | Align promise and payoff |

### The Complete Diagnostic Process

**Step 1: Global Analysis (Foolscap)**
- Identify genre(s)
- Map Beginning Hook, Middle Build, Ending Payoff
- Locate Five Commandments at global level

**Step 2: Scene Analysis (Spreadsheet)**
- Log every scene with all columns
- Identify value shifts and polarity changes
- Note turning point types (action vs. revelation)

**Step 3: Pattern Recognition**
- Look for missing or weak elements
- Identify repetitive patterns
- Find continuity issues

**Step 4: Problem Prioritization**
1. Global-level problems (most impactful)
2. Act/sequence problems
3. Scene-level problems (least impactful individually)

**Step 5: Revision Planning**
- Address big holes first
- Work systematically through problem list
- Re-analyze after major changes

## 12. CONTROLLING IDEA / THEME

### Definition

> "The Controlling Idea is a single sentence that says how the Core Value changes and why the Core Value changes from beginning to end." (PRIMARY SOURCE: Story Grid)

### Three Requirements

1. Must be boiled down to the fewest possible words (one sentence)
2. Must describe the climactic value charge (positive or negative)
3. Must be specific about the cause of the change

### Formula

**[VALUE] prevails when we choose [ACTION TAKEN BY PROTAGONIST]**

### Building the Statement

1. Identify the genre's core value
2. Determine if ending is positive or negative
3. Identify what choice/action caused the outcome
4. Combine into single statement

### Examples by Genre

| Genre | Positive Controlling Idea | Negative Controlling Idea |
|-------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Action** | Life prevails when we are willing to sacrifice | Death triumphs when we act selfishly |
| **Love** | Love prevails when lovers sacrifice | Love is lost when lovers fail to commit |
| **Status** | Success comes from staying true to values | Failure results from selling out |

## CONSOLIDATED FRAMEWORK: SHAWN COYNE'S STORY GRID

### Core Principles (Direct from Coyne)

1. **Stories are about change** - Every unit must show value shift
2. **Genre determines expectations** - Know your conventions and obligatory scenes
3. **The Five Commandments are universal** - They appear in every story unit
4. **Diagnosis before prescription** - Find ALL problems before fixing
5. **The reader matters most** - Never cheat on genre obligations
6. **Scenes are building blocks** - If scenes don't work, story doesn't work
7. **Values must shift** - No shift = no scene = no story

### Story Grid Diagnostic Process

**STEP 1: IDENTIFY GENRE**
- Use Five-Leaf Clover (Time, Structure, Reality, Style, Content)
- Content genre is most important
- Identify both external and internal genres
- List obligatory scenes and conventions

**STEP 2: MAP GLOBAL STRUCTURE**
- Complete Foolscap Global Story Grid
- Identify Beginning Hook (25%), Middle Build (50%), Ending Payoff (25%)
- Locate Five Commandments at global level
- Draft controlling idea

**STEP 3: ANALYZE SCENES**
- Complete Story Grid Spreadsheet for every scene
- Track: value shift, polarity, turning point type
- Verify Five Commandments present in each scene
- Check for variety in turning points

**STEP 4: IDENTIFY PROBLEMS**
- Missing obligatory scenes?
- Conventions not established?
- Scenes without value shifts?
- Repetitive turning points?
- Inciting incident doesn't match climax?
- Crisis doesn't force true choice?

**STEP 5: PRIORITIZE AND FIX**
- Global problems first
- Sequence/act problems second
- Scene problems third
- Re-analyze after major changes

### Quality Criteria (Excellent Story)

- [ ] Clear genre classification (external + internal)
- [ ] All obligatory scenes present and innovative
- [ ] All conventions established
- [ ] Five Commandments in every scene
- [ ] Value shifts in every scene
- [ ] Variety in turning points (action AND revelation)
- [ ] Inciting incident promises climax
- [ ] Crisis forces true dilemma
- [ ] Controlling idea is clear and supported
- [ ] Beginning hooks, middle builds, ending pays off

### Quality Criteria (Weak Story)

- [ ] Genre unclear or mixed without purpose
- [ ] Missing obligatory scenes
- [ ] Conventions not established
- [ ] Scenes without value change
- [ ] All turning points same type
- [ ] Inciting incident disconnected from climax
- [ ] Crisis is no-brainer decision
- [ ] Ending doesn't pay off setup
- [ ] Reader expectations violated
