## 3. The Sparkline: Theoretical Foundation

### What Is the Sparkline?

The Sparkline is Nancy Duarte's analytical and planning tool for mapping the structure of persuasive presentations. It's a graphical representation that shows how a presentation moves between describing "what is" (current reality) and "what could be" (desired future state).

**Definition:**
> "A sparkline is a graphical representation of a presentation that shows the points at which it moves between describing 'what is' to describing 'what could be.'" (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### The Core Insight

Duarte discovered that the most persuasive presentations oscillate between two horizontal planes:
1. **Lower plane:** "What Is" - the current situation, status quo, problems
2. **Upper plane:** "What Could Be" - the desired future, vision, solutions

**Key Pattern:**
> "Throughout these talks the speaker contrasts between 'what is the current situation' and 'what could be' repeatedly." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### Visual Representation

```
                    What Could Be (Vision/Future)
                    ↑     ↑     ↑     ↑     ↑
                    │     │     │     │     │
     ──┬──┬──┬──┬──┼──┼──┼──┼──┼──┼──┼──→ Time
       │  │  │  │  │     │     │     │
       ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓     ↓     ↓     ↓
                    What Is (Current Reality)

The line oscillates between the two planes throughout the presentation,
ending on a high plane with "The New Bliss"
```

### Why It Works

The Sparkline works because:
1. **Contrast creates engagement:** 60-80% of listeners report higher engagement when talks alternate contrastively rather than linearly
2. **Tension and release:** Building and releasing tension keeps audiences engaged
3. **Emotional journey:** Creates peaks and valleys that mirror the hero's journey
4. **Clear direction:** Shows the gap between now and the desired future

**Research Finding:**
> "60-80% of listeners report higher engagement when talks alternate contrastively rather than linearly." (PRIMARY - Duarte's reported findings)

## 4. The "What Is" vs "What Could Be" Framework

### The Core Oscillation

This is the beating heart of Duarte's methodology. Every word and piece of information in a presentation can be categorized as either "what is" (today) or "what could be" (tomorrow).

### "What Is" Segments

**Purpose:** Describe the current situation, status quo, challenges, pain points, or risks.

**Characteristics:**
- The way things are today
- What needs to change
- Problems the audience faces
- Current frustrations
- The status quo

**How to Deliver:**
- Use the audience's language and perspective
- Interview stakeholders beforehand to understand their world
- Be specific about challenges they face
- Demonstrate you understand their reality

**Key Quote:**
> "Accurately capturing the current reality and sentiments of the audience's world demonstrates that you have experience and insights on their situation." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

### "What Could Be" Segments

**Purpose:** Describe the desired future, vision, benefits, solutions, or rewards.

**Characteristics:**
- The potential future state
- What success looks like
- Benefits of adoption
- Solutions to problems
- The transformed reality

**How to Deliver:**
- Paint a vivid picture of success
- Focus on concrete benefits and outcomes
- Use sensory language
- Make it emotionally compelling, not just logically sound

**Key Quote:**
> "Create a stark contrast between the status quo ('what is') and the future they desire ('what could be'). Paint a vivid picture of what success looks like after implementing your idea or solution." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

### The Toggle Technique

**Critical Principle:**
> "Deliberately alternate between describing pain points and their corresponding solutions throughout your presentation, rather than grouping all problems at the beginning and solutions at the end." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

**Pattern:**
```
WHAT IS → WHAT COULD BE → WHAT IS → WHAT COULD BE → WHAT IS → WHAT COULD BE → NEW BLISS
(Problem)   (Solution)    (Problem)   (Solution)    (Problem)   (Solution)    (Vision)
```

### Building the Gap

The power comes from making the gap visible:
1. Toggle between states to create the BIGGEST possible gap
2. Demonstrate how unappealing the status quo is
3. Show how incredibly bright the future looks
4. The toggling breaks down resistance to the idea

**Key Insight:**
> "The toggling helps to break down any possible resistance to the idea." (PRIMARY - Duarte methodology)

## 5. Three-Act Structure for Presentations

### Overview

Duarte's framework follows a narrative arc with a clear beginning, middle, and end, separated by two turning points.

### The Complete Structure

```
BEGINNING ─────→ TURNING POINT 1 ─────→ MIDDLE ─────→ TURNING POINT 2 ─────→ END
(≤10%)          (Call to Adventure)     (Contrast)     (Call to Action)      (New Bliss)
```

### Act I: The Beginning (≤10% of Time)

**Purpose:** Paint a picture of the audience's current reality and establish shared understanding.

**What to Do:**
- Set the baseline of "what is"
- Deliver a concise formulation of what everyone agrees is true
- Connect with the audience's world
- Demonstrate empathy and understanding
- Build common ground

**Key Requirement:**
> "This should not take more than 10% of your total time." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

**Critical Rule:**
> "It has to be empathetic, it has to be about the audience." (PRIMARY - Nancy Duarte)

### Turning Point 1: The Call to Adventure

**Purpose:** Create an imbalance by introducing "what could be" for the first time.

**What to Do:**
- Juxtapose "what could be" against "what is"
- Put forth a memorable Big Idea
- Create the first stark contrast
- Jolt the audience from complacency
- Make the gap explicit, not muddled or vague

**Key Quote:**
> "The first turning point should show the audience a gap between what is and what could be – jolting the audience from complacency." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### Act II: The Middle (Majority of Time)

**Purpose:** Present contrasting content, alternating between "what is" and "what could be."

**What to Do:**
- Move back and forth between states
- Create and resolve tension through contrast
- Build toward the climax
- Include S.T.A.R. moments
- Stack proof and evidence

**Structure Within the Middle:**
```
WHAT IS → WHAT COULD BE → WHAT IS → WHAT COULD BE → (repeat)
```

**Key Quote:**
> "The middle moves up and down as if something new is constantly happening. This back and forth structural motion pushes the audience to feel as if events are constantly unfolding." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### Turning Point 2: The Call to Action

**Purpose:** Clearly define what you're asking the audience to do.

**What to Do:**
- Clearly articulate what the audience can do
- Include discrete tasks to move from "what is" to "what could be"
- Create urgency
- Make the action specific and immediate

**Key Quote:**
> "Turning point 2: call to action: clearly define what you're asking the audience to do." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

**Warning:**
> "Ending a presentation with a to-do list is not inspirational." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

### Act III: The End (The New Bliss)

**Purpose:** End on a higher plane with a vivid picture of the transformed future.

**What to Do:**
- Describe the world with your idea fully adopted
- Help audience engage in outcome visualization
- Describe potential future outcomes with wonder and awe
- Leave audiences inspired
- End higher than you began

**Definition of New Bliss:**
> "This is a depiction of the world where the audience has fully embraced your Big Idea, the central point of your presentation." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

**Examples:**
- Steve Jobs ended the iPhone launch with the promise that Apple will continue to build revolutionary new products
- Martin Luther King Jr. ended with "Free at last! Free at last! Thank God Almighty, we are free at last!"

**Key Quote:**
> "At the very end, you need to describe the world as a new bliss, this is utopia with my idea adopted, this is the way the world is going to look, when we join together and we solve this big problem." (PRIMARY - Nancy Duarte, TED Talk)

## 6. The Big Idea Concept

### What Is The Big Idea?

The Big Idea is the single, central message of your presentation—a complete sentence that conveys what's at stake and motivates audience adoption.

**Definition:**
> "Your big idea is that one key message you want to communicate. It contains the impetus that compels the audience to set a new course with a new compass heading." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

**Alternative Names:**
- Controlling idea (screenwriting)
- Thesis statement
- Take-away
- Gist
- Single unifying message

### Requirements for a Big Idea

1. **Must be a complete sentence** - Not just a topic
2. **Contains a noun and a verb**
3. **Ideally includes "you"** - Audience-centric
4. **Conveys what's at stake** - Both pleasure (if accepted) and pain (if rejected)
5. **Stands out through contrast** - Distinct from existing expectations

**Bad Example:**
> "It's about new software" - This is a topic, not a Big Idea

**Good Example:**
> "This software will generate $1M in revenue over two years" - This is a Big Idea

**Another Example:**
> "The fate of the oceans" is a topic. "Worldwide pollution is killing the ocean and us" is a Big Idea. (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### The Big Idea Formula

**Two Parts:**

1. **Part 1:** A unique stance and clear perspective that makes you distinct, specific, and relevant
2. **Part 2:** What the audience can expect if they say no to what you're proposing

### Al Gore's Big Idea

**Example:**
> "Global warming is real, manmade, and its effects will be cataclysmic if we don't act now." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

Every slide and every word in "An Inconvenient Truth" supports this single message.

### How to Use the Big Idea

1. Work with the Big Idea formula FIRST
2. Then craft the rest of your talk around it
3. Use it as a filter for all elements
4. All facts, anecdotes, images, and slides should support it
5. **Cut anything that doesn't support The Big Idea** - It will only weaken your presentation

**Key Quote:**
> "Cut anything that doesn't support The Big Idea. It will only weaken your presentation." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

## 7. S.T.A.R. Moments

### What Is a S.T.A.R. Moment?

S.T.A.R. stands for "Something They'll Always Remember." It's a special moment in a presentation that magnifies the main idea and becomes what the audience remembers and talks about afterward.

**Definition:**
> "If you were to ask yourself, 'When someone leaves my talk, what do I want them to talk about?' The answer to that is your S.T.A.R. moment." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### Requirements for S.T.A.R. Moments

- **Significant:** Important to the message
- **Sincere:** Authentic, not manipulative
- **Enlightening:** Provides genuine insight
- **Drives the Big Idea home** - Not a distraction from it
- **Repeatable:** Easy to describe and share
- **Planned:** Don't happen accidentally

**Key Quote:**
> "This should be a powerful, genuine, and educational moment in the presentation that magnifies your idea." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### The Five Types of S.T.A.R. Moments

#### 1. Memorable Dramatization

Small dramatizations that convey insights through props, demos, or physical demonstrations.

**Characteristics:**
- Props that illustrate a point
- Live demonstrations
- Reenactments or skits
- Physical actions

**Famous Example:**
> Steve Jobs in 2008 pulled the MacBook Air from a manila envelope to demonstrate how thin it was. (PRIMARY - Resonate)

**Another Example:**
> Bill Gates released mosquitoes into the TED audience, stating, "There's no reason only poor people should have this experience." This drove home the seriousness of malaria more powerfully than any statistic could. (PRIMARY - Resonate)

#### 2. Repeatable Sound Bites

Small, quotable phrases that audiences can easily remember and repeat.

**Characteristics:**
- Slogans and catchphrases
- Headlines for the press
- Rally cries for employees
- Social media-ready content

**Techniques:**
- Imitation
- Word repetition at beginning, middle, or end of sentences
- Delivered with strategic pauses
- Visual reinforcement

#### 3. Evocative Visuals

Dramatic images that fill the screen and create emotional connections.

**Characteristics:**
- Full-screen images
- Hauntingly beautiful or striking imagery
- Minimal text
- Emotional resonance

**Example:**
> Lisa Kristine's hauntingly beautiful images of enslaved people throughout the world. (PRIMARY - Resonate)

#### 4. Emotive Storytelling

Personal narratives attached to core ideas that help audiences feel what you feel.

**Characteristics:**
- Personal stories
- Emotional authenticity
- Relatable experiences
- Connection to the Big Idea

**Why It Works:**
> "When you tell a story, all the sensing parts of their brains light up and information gets packaged in a way people will remember and be able to recall and retell." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

**Key Quote:**
> "People will always remember how you made them feel." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

#### 5. Shocking Statistics

Numbers presented in ways that make them comprehensible and memorable.

**Characteristics:**
- Tie complex numbers to familiar things
- Provide scale and context
- Make abstract numbers concrete
- Use comparisons

**Why Needed:**
> "The scale of numbers we work with are almost impossible to comprehend. They're vast. Millions, billions, and trillions are nowhere near each other in scale. Instead, tie complex numbers to something people are familiar with." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### Planning S.T.A.R. Moments

**Key Insight:**
> "S.T.A.R. moments don't accidentally happen, they need to be thought through and planned which takes creative energy." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

**Process:**
1. Identify your key messages
2. Spend creative energy thinking of out-of-the-box ways to make points stick
3. Consider your audience's preferences and sensitivities
4. Ensure the moment embodies significance, sincerity, and enlightenment
5. Test that it's easily repeatable/describable

## 8. Audience as Hero, Presenter as Mentor

### The Core Philosophy

Nancy Duarte upends the traditional paradigm by positioning the audience as the hero of the story, not the presenter.

**Key Quote:**
> "The core philosophy is that your audience is the hero—not you, your company, or your product." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### The Problem with Traditional Presentations

Most presentations start with "me-ness"—focusing on the company, the presenter, or the product. This self-centered approach alienates the audience.

**Key Quote:**
> "Duarte critically points out that the presenter is not the hero; the audience is. Most presentations mistakenly start with 'me-ness'—focusing on the company or presenter. This self-centered approach alienates the audience." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### The Presenter as Mentor

The presenter must take on the role of the mentor—like Yoda, not Luke Skywalker.

**Famous Mentor Examples:**
- Yoda to Luke Skywalker
- Dumbledore to Harry Potter
- Glinda to Dorothy
- Morpheus to Neo

### What Mentors Provide

| What Mentors Offer | Example |
|-------------------|---------|
| Guidance | Showing the path forward |
| Confidence | Believing in the hero's ability |
| Insight | Revealing hidden truths |
| Advice | Specific recommendations |
| Training | Building skills |
| "Magical gifts" | Tools to overcome challenges |

**Key Quote:**
> "As the mentor (like Yoda), the presenter's role is to provide guidance, confidence, insight, advice, training, or 'magical gifts' to help the audience overcome fears and embark on a new journey." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### Transformation as the Goal

The ultimate purpose is transformation—moving the audience from one state to another.

**Key Quote:**
> "The hero's journey—and presentations—are all about transformation. As a presenter, you want your hero-filled audience to transform in some way by the end of your talk." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### The Role of Conflict

Like any good story, presentations need an antagonist—barriers the hero faces.

**Types of Antagonists in Business Presentations:**
- Competitors
- Market challenges
- Internal resistance
- Outdated thinking
- Fear of change

### Practical Application

1. **Begin with empathy** - Understand the audience's world before trying to change it
2. **Position yourself as a guide** - Not the hero of your own story
3. **Give them tools** - Help them overcome their specific challenges
4. **Focus on their transformation** - What will change for them?

## 9. Types of Contrast

### Why Contrast Matters

Contrast is what keeps audiences engaged. Without contrast, presentations become monotonous and lose attention.

**Key Quote:**
> "Contrast (or conflict) keeps listeners engaged—it is what inches you closer to the edge of your seat during that awe-inspiring keynote speech. Our brains need contrast to stay engaged." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

### Three Types of Contrast

Nancy Duarte identifies three types of contrast that create energy in presentations:

#### 1. Content Contrast

Moving back and forth to compare different perspectives.

**Types:**
- What is vs. What could be
- Your views vs. Audience's views
- Problem vs. Solution
- Advantages vs. Disadvantages
- Cause vs. Effect
- Compare vs. Contrast

**Key Quote:**
> "Content contrast moves back and forth to compare what is to what could be - and your views versus the audience's." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

#### 2. Emotion Contrast

Alternating between analytical and emotional content.

**Types:**
- Stories vs. Data
- Heart vs. Mind
- Qualitative vs. Quantitative
- Anecdotes vs. Metrics

**Application:**
> "Balance qualitative content (stories, examples, and anecdotes) with quantitative content (hard data and metrics). This way, you'll appeal to the audience's minds and hearts." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

#### 3. Delivery Contrast

Moving between different presentation methods.

**Types:**
- Traditional vs. Nontraditional
- Standing vs. Moving
- Speaking vs. Showing
- Slides vs. Props
- Solo vs. Interactive

### Building Tension Through Contrast

**Structure:**
> "Whatever type of narrative contrast you choose, leverage it to build and release tension, ebbing and flowing back and forth between two opposing states." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

**Result:**
> "Building contrasting elements into your presentation helps hold an audience's attention. They enjoy experiencing a small dilemma, and then learning its resolution." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

## 10. Visual Storytelling Principles

### From slide:ology

Nancy Duarte's visual design principles revolutionized presentation design.

### Core Principles

#### One Idea Per Slide

Convert bullet-riddled slides into individual slides, each with one clear message.

**Rationale:**
> "Since people can't multitask, audiences cannot simultaneously read dense, bullet-riddled slides AND listen to you AND understand what you mean." (PRIMARY - slide:ology)

#### Three-Second Comprehension Test

The audience should be able to grasp the meaning of each slide within three seconds.

**Key Quote:**
> "Your slide should function more like a billboard than any other media. The audience should be able to quickly grasp the meaning of the slide before turning back to the presenter." (PRIMARY - slide:ology)

#### Images Over Text

Replace text-heavy content with visuals wherever possible.

**Techniques:**
- Use stock photos with speech bubbles for narrative
- Use diagrams instead of bullet points
- Let images tell the story

#### Slide Frequency

Change slides at least every two minutes to maintain engagement.

**Guideline:** Only include elements that support message recall.

### CRAP Design Principles

Duarte advocates for the CRAP framework for visual design:

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **C**ontrast | Make different elements look different |
| **R**epetition | Repeat visual elements for consistency |
| **A**lignment | Line up elements intentionally |
| **P**roximity | Group related items together |

## 11. Case Study Analyses

### Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" Speech

Duarte's analysis of King's 16-minute speech revealed perfect adherence to the Sparkline structure.

**Key Findings:**
- About 20% of King's speech was metaphorical
- He alternated between "what is" (harsh realities of discrimination) and "what could be" (his dream of a harmonious society)
- Used poetic repetition, pacing, and visual metaphors
- The speech was almost musical, using cadence to create suspense
- Famous lines ("I have a dream...") occur where there are fluctuations in the structure

**The Metaphor:**
> "He likened his lack of freedom to a bad check that America has given the Negro people. King introduced his metaphor three minutes into his 16-minute talk, and it was the first time the audience roared and clapped." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

**Structure:**
> "Martin Luther King Jr. starts with what is, moves back and forth between what is and what could be, and ends with a very poetic new bliss, which is the famous part we all know." (PRIMARY - Nancy Duarte, TED Talk)

### Steve Jobs's iPhone Launch (2007)

Duarte analyzed Jobs's 90-minute iPhone presentation as a masterclass in the Sparkline.

**Key Findings:**
- Maintained a heightened sense of "what could be"
- Created multiple S.T.A.R. moments throughout
- Each feature reveal was a peak in the Sparkline
- Ended with the "New Bliss" - Apple continuing to build revolutionary products

**Structure:**
> "Duarte combines these frameworks into a sparkline, like the one for the iPhone announcement by Steve Jobs. Time is demarcated along the x-axis. Along the y-axis, the sparkline shows whether the presenter is talking about the present or the future." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### Al Gore's "An Inconvenient Truth"

Duarte's firm created the presentation for this Oscar-winning documentary.

**S.T.A.R. Moment:**
> "The presentation was filled with STAR Moments, such as the dramatic use of a cherry picker to show the scale of CO2 emissions over time. This visual and emotional impact of Gore's narrative created a compelling call to action that resonated with audiences around the world." (PRIMARY - Duarte case study)

**Big Idea:**
> "Global warming is real, manmade, and its effects will be cataclysmic if we don't act now." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

### The Identical Structure Discovery

**Key Quote:**
> "Nancy discovered that Steve Jobs' iPhone pitch speech and Martin Luther King, Jr.'s 'I Have a Dream' speech have the identical underlying structure that made their speeches so memorable." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

## 12. Practical Application Framework

### Step-by-Step Process for Crafting a TED-Style Talk

#### Step 1: Define Your Big Idea

1. Write a single, complete sentence
2. Include what's at stake
3. Make it audience-centric (include "you")
4. Test: Does everything support this one idea?

#### Step 2: Analyze Your Audience

Use Duarte's seven-dimension framework:

| Dimension | Questions to Ask |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Identity** | Childhood experiences, psychological shaping? |
| **Lifestyle** | Preferred spaces, daily patterns? |
| **Values** | Priorities, time/money allocation? |
| **Knowledge** | Existing expertise, information sources, biases? |
| **Influence** | Decision-makers, past experiences? |
| **Motivation** | Desires, gaps, intrinsic drivers? |
| **Respect** | How they give/receive recognition? |

#### Step 3: Structure Your Sparkline

**Beginning (≤10%):**
- Establish common ground
- Describe their current reality
- Build empathy

**Turning Point 1:**
- Introduce your Big Idea
- Create the first contrast
- Make the gap explicit

**Middle (Majority):**
- Alternate between What Is and What Could Be
- Include at least one S.T.A.R. Moment
- Build and release tension

**Turning Point 2:**
- Deliver your Call to Action
- Be specific about what they should do

**End (New Bliss):**
- Paint the picture of the transformed future
- End on an inspirational note
- Leave them visualizing success

#### Step 4: Plan Your S.T.A.R. Moments

1. Identify 1-3 key messages to amplify
2. Choose the type (dramatization, sound bite, visual, story, statistic)
3. Plan the execution carefully
4. Test for repeatability

#### Step 5: Design Visual Support

1. One idea per slide
2. Three-second comprehension test
3. Images over text
4. Change slides every 2 minutes maximum

### Content Development Process (Five Steps)

**From Resonate:**

1. **Generate ideas** - Research, contrast exploration, personal stories, data-to-meaning translation
2. **Filter best ideas** - Keep only what supports the Big Idea; cluster by topic (3-5 supporting ideas each)
3. **Convert to charged messages** - Turn clusters into compelling sentences; arrange for maximum impact
4. **Verify contrast** - Check for content, emotional, and delivery contrast
5. **Design visuals** - One idea per slide, prioritize images over text

### Timing Guidelines

| Section | Percentage | For 18-min TED Talk |
|---------|------------|---------------------|
| Beginning | ≤10% | ~2 minutes |
| Middle | ~80% | ~14 minutes |
| End | ~10% | ~2 minutes |

**Pacing Tip:**
> "You can reduce the time between each toggle (as Nancy demonstrates in King's speech) to create a heightened level of excitement." (PRIMARY - Duarte)

## 13. Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns

### What Nancy Duarte Would NEVER Do

| Anti-Pattern | Why It's Wrong | The Fix |
|--------------|----------------|---------|
| **Start with "me-ness"** | Alienates audience | Start with audience's world |
| **All problems, then solutions** | No contrast, loses attention | Toggle throughout |
| **No Big Idea** | Unfocused, unmemorable | Morpheus single sentence thesis |
| **No S.T.A.R. moments** | Forgettable | Plan memorable peaks |
| **Bullet-point heavy slides** | Can't read and listen | One idea per slide |
| **End with to-do list** | Not inspirational | End with New Bliss vision |
| **Linear progression** | Monotonous | Create contrast |
| **Vague call to action** | No behavior change | Be specific about next step |
| **Presenter as hero** | Self-centered | Audience is hero, you're mentor |
| **Data without story** | Doesn't resonate | Use DataStory principles |

### Common Presentation Mistakes

1. **Losing audience in first 30 seconds**
   > "Many presenters lose their audience within the first 30 seconds. If that happens, it's as if you never even presented." (PRIMARY - Nancy Duarte, Twitter/X)

2. **Not understanding your role**
   > "So many people feel like they're the central figure—kind of like the hero of the story—because they're the one talking the most. But in reality, your role is that of a mentor." (PRIMARY - Nancy Duarte)

3. **Not making the gap clear**
   > "It's crucial that the gap is clear. The turning point should be explicit, not muddled or vague." (PRIMARY - Resonate)

4. **Not being audience-centric**
   > "The number one thing is to be audience-centric—to take the time to think through who the audience is and develop all your material from a place of empathy toward them." (PRIMARY - Nancy Duarte, TED Blog)
