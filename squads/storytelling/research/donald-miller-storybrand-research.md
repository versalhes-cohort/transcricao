This research provides complete documentation of the SB7 framework, including all seven elements, the three levels of problems, guide positioning (empathy + authority), plan types, CTAs, stakes, success outcomes, and practical implementation tools like the BrandScript and One-Liner.

## 2. THE SB7 FRAMEWORK - COMPLETE ANATOMY

### Core Philosophy (PRIMARY)

> "Your business is not the hero of your brand story. Your customer is."
> — Donald Miller, Building a StoryBrand

> "Pretty websites don't sell things. Words sell things. And if we haven't clarified our message, our customers won't listen."
> — Donald Miller

> "If you confuse, you'll lose."
> — StoryBrand Mantra

### The 7 Principles of StoryBrand (PRIMARY)

| # | Principle | Explanation |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | The customer is the hero, not your brand | Position customer as protagonist |
| 2 | Companies sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to internal problems | Address emotional needs, not just practical |
| 3 | Customers aren't looking for another hero; they're looking for a guide | Be Yoda, not Luke |
| 4 | Customers trust a guide who has a plan | Reduce perceived risk with clear steps |
| 5 | Customers do not take action unless challenged | Include clear calls to action |
| 6 | Every human being is trying to avoid a tragic ending | Communicate stakes |
| 7 | Never assume people understand how your brand can change their lives | Tell them explicitly |

### The 7 Elements of the SB7 Formula (PRIMARY)

```
1. CHARACTER (Hero) → The customer wants something
2. PROBLEM → They encounter a problem stopping them
3. GUIDE → Your brand steps in as the guide
4. PLAN → The guide provides a plan
5. CALL TO ACTION → The guide calls them to act
6. FAILURE (Stakes) → What they stand to lose
7. SUCCESS → What they might gain
```

### Story Structure Visualization

```
A CHARACTER who wants something
    ↓
encounters a PROBLEM before they can get it
    ↓
At the peak of their despair, a GUIDE steps into their lives
    ↓
gives them a PLAN
    ↓
and CALLS THEM TO ACTION
    ↓
That action helps them avoid FAILURE
    ↓
and ends in a SUCCESS
```

## 3. ELEMENT 1: CHARACTER (THE HERO)

### Definition

The Character is your customer - they are the hero of the story, not your brand. The first step is to clearly define what your customer WANTS.

### Key Rules (PRIMARY)

1. **Identify ONE clear want** - Not multiple, not vague
2. **Make it specific and tangible** - Something they can visualize
3. **Create a "story gap"** - Distance between where they are and where they want to be
4. **Focus on survival-related desires** - Things connected to thriving

### Customer Wants Categories

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Conserve resources** | Save time, save money, reduce effort |
| **Social status** | Be respected, admired, connected |
| **Accumulate resources** | Make money, gain skills, acquire |
| **Meaning & purpose** | Contribute, make a difference |
| **Safety** | Feel secure, protected, stable |
| **Love & belonging** | Connection, community, acceptance |

### Character Definition Template

```yaml
character:
  who: "[Specific description of your ideal customer]"
  wants: "[One clear, specific desire]"
  current_state: "[Where they are now]"
  desired_state: "[Where they want to be]"
  story_gap: "[The distance between current and desired]"
```

### Examples

**Financial Advisor:**
- Who: Professionals in their 40s-50s approaching retirement
- Wants: Financial security and freedom in retirement
- Story Gap: Uncertainty about whether they have enough saved

**Meal Delivery Service:**
- Who: Busy moms who want their families to eat healthier
- Wants: Healthy meals without the time investment
- Story Gap: No time to shop and cook healthy meals

## 4. ELEMENT 2: PROBLEM (Three Levels)

### The Three Levels of Problems (PRIMARY)

This is one of Miller's most important insights: **"Companies tend to sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to internal problems."**

#### Level 1: EXTERNAL PROBLEM

**Definition:** The tangible, surface-level problem that can be pointed to.

**Characteristics:**
- Observable and measurable
- What you might think you're solving
- Often the "obvious" problem
- Usually tied to a physical or practical need

**Examples:**
| Business | External Problem |
|----------|------------------|
| Real Estate | Need more space for a growing family |
| IT Services | Computer systems are slow and outdated |
| Fitness Coach | Want to lose 20 pounds |
| Financial Advisor | Don't have a retirement plan |

#### Level 2: INTERNAL PROBLEM

**Definition:** The emotional challenges and feelings the external problem creates.

**Characteristics:**
- How the external problem makes them FEEL
- Often frustration, fear, confusion, overwhelm, doubt
- This is what they ACTUALLY buy solutions for
- The emotional weight of the problem

**Examples:**
| Business | Internal Problem |
|----------|------------------|
| Real Estate | Stressed about change, doubting they can find what they want |
| IT Services | Frustrated, embarrassed by system failures in front of clients |
| Fitness Coach | Feel insecure, ashamed of how they look |
| Financial Advisor | Anxious about the future, worried they'll be a burden |

#### Level 3: PHILOSOPHICAL PROBLEM

**Definition:** Why this matters in the grand scheme of things. The "ought" or "should" statement.

**Characteristics:**
- Connects to larger sense of right/wrong
- Why the story MATTERS
- Often starts with "People should..." or "It's not right that..."
- Elevates the problem to something meaningful

**Examples:**
| Business | Philosophical Problem |
|----------|----------------------|
| Real Estate | Everyone deserves a home that fits their life |
| IT Services | Technology should make life easier, not harder |
| Fitness Coach | People deserve to feel confident in their own bodies |
| Financial Advisor | You should be able to enjoy what you've worked for |

### The Villain (Problem Personified)

In story terms, the PROBLEM often takes the form of a VILLAIN. Miller advises giving the problem a "face" or personifying it.

**Good Villains:**
- Are a root source of the problem
- Are relatable to your audience
- Have a single, clear identity
- Are real (not abstract concepts)

**Example Villains:**
- High fees (for financial services)
- Complexity (for technology)
- Time thieves (for productivity tools)
- Wasted money (for efficiency solutions)

### Problem Section Template

```yaml
problem:
  villain: "[Personified root cause of problem]"
  external: "[Tangible, observable problem]"
  internal: "[How it makes them feel]"
  philosophical: "[Why it matters - the 'ought' statement]"
```

## 5. ELEMENT 3: GUIDE (Empathy + Authority)

### The Core Concept (PRIMARY)

> "Customers aren't looking for another hero; they're looking for a guide."
> — Donald Miller

Your brand is NOT the hero. Your brand is the GUIDE - like Yoda to Luke Skywalker, or Gandalf to Frodo.

### The Two Characteristics of an Effective Guide

A guide MUST demonstrate BOTH:

#### 1. EMPATHY

**Definition:** A deep understanding of the customer's problem and a sincere desire to help.

**What Empathy IS:**
- Understanding their struggles
- Acknowledging their feelings
- Showing you "get it"
- Demonstrating you've been there or helped others there

**What Empathy IS NOT:**
- Pity
- Talking about yourself
- Dismissing their concerns
- Generic "we care" statements

**Empathy Statement Formula:**
> "We understand what it's like to [specific struggle]."

**Examples:**
| Business | Empathy Statement |
|----------|-------------------|
| Financial Advisor | "We know how overwhelming it can feel to plan for retirement when there are so many unknowns" |
| IT Services | "We understand the frustration of technology that's supposed to help but ends up wasting your time" |
| Fitness Coach | "We know what it's like to look in the mirror and not recognize yourself anymore" |

#### 2. AUTHORITY

**Definition:** Demonstrated competence that proves you can solve the problem.

**Ways to Demonstrate Authority:**
| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Testimonials** | Customer success stories, video testimonials |
| **Statistics** | "Helped 10,000+ businesses", "97% success rate" |
| **Awards** | Industry recognition, certifications |
| **Logos** | Client logos, media appearances |
| **Experience** | Years in business, projects completed |
| **Credentials** | Certifications, degrees, partnerships |

**Authority Statement Formula:**
> "[Credential/proof] that demonstrates [competence relevant to their problem]."

**Examples:**
| Business | Authority Statement |
|----------|---------------------|
| Financial Advisor | "For 20 years, we've helped over 500 families retire with confidence" |
| IT Services | "Certified by Microsoft, we've completed 1,000+ successful implementations" |
| Fitness Coach | "Certified trainer who's helped 2,000 clients lose a combined 50,000 pounds" |

### Balance is Critical (PRIMARY)

**Too much empathy, no authority:** "You seem nice, but can you actually help me?"
**Too much authority, no empathy:** "You're impressive, but do you understand my situation?"

The guide must show BOTH to earn trust.

### Guide Section Template

```yaml
guide:
  empathy:
    statement: "[We understand what it's like to...]"
    proof: "[How you demonstrate understanding]"
  authority:
    type: "[testimonials, statistics, awards, experience]"
    proof: "[Specific credentials or evidence]"
    statement: "[Authority positioning statement]"
```

**Source:** [Studio Founded](https://www.studiofounded.com/brand-materials/compelling-brand-stories-story-brand-framework), [Creativeo](https://www.creativeo.co/post/yes-your-customers-are-looking-for-a-guide-not-a-hero)

## 6. ELEMENT 4: PLAN (Process + Agreement)

### Why Plans Matter (PRIMARY)

> "Customers trust a guide who has a plan."
> — StoryBrand Principle Four

Plans serve two critical functions:
1. **Clarify HOW** to do business with you
2. **Reduce RISK** in the customer's mind

Without a plan, customers face hesitation: "What if it doesn't work?", "Will it be too complicated?", "Is it worth the risk?"

### Two Types of Plans

#### TYPE 1: PROCESS PLAN

**Purpose:** Clarify the steps to do business with you or use your product.

**Rules:**
- Keep it to **3-6 steps** (3 is ideal)
- Simple, clear language
- Show the path to success
- If more than 6 steps, combine into phases

**Pre-Purchase Process Plan Template:**
```
1. [First step - usually engagement/discovery]
2. [Second step - usually customization/planning]
3. [Third step - usually execution/results]
```

**Examples:**

**Financial Advisor:**
1. Schedule a discovery call
2. We create your personalized retirement plan
3. Watch your wealth grow with confidence

**IT Services:**
1. Book your free tech audit
2. We implement your custom solution
3. Enjoy stress-free technology

**Real Estate:**
1. Tell us about your dream home
2. We show you only homes that match
3. Move into your perfect home

**Post-Purchase Process Plan Template:**
```
1. [Onboarding/setup step]
2. [Integration/learning step]
3. [Success/results step]
```

#### TYPE 2: AGREEMENT PLAN

**Purpose:** Alleviate fears and reduce perceived risk.

**Rules:**
- Address the customer's INTERNAL fears
- Make promises that overcome objections
- Can be displayed publicly or used in sales conversations
- Focus on risk reduction and shared values

**Agreement Plan Types:**

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Risk Reduction** | Reduce fear of bad outcome | "Satisfaction guaranteed or your money back" |
| **Quality Assurance** | Prove competence | "Every product tested against 50-point checklist" |
| **Shared Values** | Build trust through alignment | "We only recommend what we'd use ourselves" |

**Examples:**

**Financial Advisor Agreement Plan:**
- We always act as a fiduciary (your interests first)
- No hidden fees, ever
- You'll understand every recommendation we make
- 24/7 access to your accounts and advisors

**IT Services Agreement Plan:**
- Same-day response guarantee
- No geek-speak - plain English only
- Fixed pricing - no surprise bills
- We don't leave until it works

### Naming Your Plan (PRIMARY)

> "Give your plan a name to increase its perceived value."
> — Donald Miller

**Good Plan Names:**
- "The Quick Start Plan"
- "The Peace of Mind Package"
- "The Easy Installation Process"
- "Our Customer Success Promise"

### Plan Section Template

```yaml
plan:
  process:
    name: "[Name of your process plan]"
    steps:
      - step_1: "[First step]"
      - step_2: "[Second step]"
      - step_3: "[Third step]"
  agreement:
    name: "[Name of your agreement/guarantee]"
    promises:
      - "[Promise 1]"
      - "[Promise 2]"
      - "[Promise 3]"
```

**Source:** [ClearBrand](https://clearbrand.com/storybrand-agreement-plan/), [Results & Co](https://resultsandco.com.au/blog/what-is-the-process-plan-of-storybrand/), [ROI Online](https://www.roionline.com/post/how-to-make-the-purchase-decision-easier-with-the-storybrand-agreement-plan)

## 7. ELEMENT 5: CALL TO ACTION (Direct + Transitional)

### Why CTAs Matter (PRIMARY)

> "Customers do not take action unless they are challenged to take action."
> — StoryBrand Principle Five

Most businesses are too passive with their CTAs. Miller uses the analogy: **"Marry me or date me"** - you need BOTH types.

### Two Types of CTAs

#### TYPE 1: DIRECT CTA ("Marry Me")

**Purpose:** Ask for the sale directly.

**Characteristics:**
- Clear, bold, specific
- Single action
- Visible and prominent
- Not hidden or apologetic

**Direct CTA Examples:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Contact us" | "Schedule Your Free Consultation" |
| "Learn more" | "Get Started Today" |
| "Submit" | "Claim Your Spot Now" |
| "Click here" | "Download Your Free Guide" |

**Rules for Direct CTAs:**
1. Use action verbs: "Get", "Start", "Schedule", "Claim"
2. Be specific about what happens next
3. Make it visually prominent (button, contrasting color)
4. Repeat it multiple times on the page
5. Don't be afraid to be bold

#### TYPE 2: TRANSITIONAL CTA ("Date Me")

**Purpose:** Capture leads who aren't ready to buy yet.

**Characteristics:**
- Low commitment
- Offers value in exchange for contact info
- Builds trust over time
- Positions you as the guide

**Transitional CTA Types:**
| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **PDF/Guide** | "Download our free retirement planning checklist" |
| **Video Series** | "Watch our 3-part video series on [topic]" |
| **Webinar** | "Register for our free masterclass" |
| **Quiz/Assessment** | "Take our 5-minute assessment" |
| **Free Trial** | "Start your 14-day free trial" |
| **Checklist** | "Get our complete [process] checklist" |

**The Key Insight:**
> "The transitional CTA positions yourself as the guide and shows you know your stuff."
> — Donald Miller

### CTA Placement Rules (PRIMARY)

1. **Header:** Direct CTA button (always visible)
2. **Throughout page:** Direct CTA repeated
3. **Sidebar/Footer:** Transitional CTA
4. **Exit intent:** Transitional CTA popup
5. **Email signature:** Transitional CTA

### CTA Section Template

```yaml
calls_to_action:
  direct:
    primary: "[Main CTA button text]"
    variations:
      - "[Alternative CTA 1]"
      - "[Alternative CTA 2]"
  transitional:
    offer: "[What you're giving away]"
    cta_text: "[Button text for lead magnet]"
    format: "[PDF, video, webinar, quiz, etc.]"
```

**Source:** [Results & Co](https://resultsandco.com.au/blog/how-to-transitional-call-to-action-lead-magnet), [Agency Boon](https://www.agencyboon.com/blog/5-things-your-storybrand-website-should-include/)

## 8. ELEMENT 6: FAILURE (Stakes)

### Why Stakes Matter (PRIMARY)

> "Every human being is trying to avoid a tragic ending."
> — StoryBrand Principle Six

> "People hate losing something more than they like winning something."
> — Loss Aversion Principle

Without stakes, there's no urgency. Without urgency, there's no action.

### The Psychology of Stakes

**Loss Aversion:** People are more motivated by what they might LOSE than what they might GAIN. This is why the failure element is essential - it creates urgency.

**The Story Question:** In every scene of a movie, the audience must know: "What's at stake for the hero?" Your marketing must answer the same question.

### Types of Stakes

| Category | Questions to Ask |
|----------|------------------|
| **Financial Loss** | What money could they lose or miss out on? |
| **Time Wasted** | What time will they never get back? |
| **Opportunity Cost** | What opportunities will they miss? |
| **Quality of Life** | How will their daily life suffer? |
| **Relationships** | How might their relationships be affected? |
| **Health** | What health consequences might occur? |
| **Status** | How might their reputation or status suffer? |

### Examples of Stakes

**Financial Advisor:**
- Without a plan, you might outlive your money
- You could miss the retirement you've worked your whole life for
- Your family might face financial stress when you're gone

**IT Services:**
- System failure could cost you thousands in lost productivity
- A data breach could destroy customer trust
- You'll keep wasting time on problems that should be solved

**Fitness Coach:**
- Your energy will continue to decline
- You'll miss out on activities with your kids
- Health problems will only get worse

### The Balance: Stakes Without Fear-Mongering (PRIMARY)

**DO:**
- Mention stakes 2-3 times in your messaging
- Make them real and relevant
- Connect to customer's actual fears
- Use to create urgency, not panic

**DON'T:**
- Overdo it (not every sentence)
- Use scare tactics
- Make exaggerated claims
- Focus only on negative

### Stakes Section Template

```yaml
failure:
  without_solution:
    - "[What bad thing will happen/continue]"
    - "[What they'll miss out on]"
    - "[How their situation will worsen]"
  emotional_impact: "[How failure will make them feel]"
  urgency_creator: "[Why they should act now]"
```

**Source:** [Bluleadz](https://www.bluleadz.com/blog/storybrand-framework-and-brandscript), [Chatterbuzz](https://www.chatterbuzzmedia.com/blog/storybrand-brand-message-guide/)

## 9. ELEMENT 7: SUCCESS (Transformation)

### Why Success Matters (PRIMARY)

> "Never assume people understand how your brand can change their lives. Tell them."
> — StoryBrand Principle Seven

The success element paints a picture of what life looks like AFTER the customer uses your product/service. It's the "happy ending" of their story.

### The Three Dimensions of Success

#### 1. EXTERNAL SUCCESS

**Definition:** The tangible, observable outcomes.

**Questions:**
- What measurable results will they achieve?
- What will change in their external world?
- What will others see?

#### 2. INTERNAL SUCCESS

**Definition:** How they will FEEL after success.

**Questions:**
- What emotions will they experience?
- What internal struggles will be resolved?
- How will their self-perception change?

#### 3. ASPIRATIONAL IDENTITY

**Definition:** Who they will BECOME.

> "The key questions brands should ask are: Who does our customer want to become? What kind of person do they want to be? What is their aspirational identity?"
> — Donald Miller

**Aspirational Identity Examples:**
| From | To |
|------|-----|
| Overwhelmed beginner | Confident expert |
| Stressed executive | Peaceful leader |
| Struggling business owner | Successful entrepreneur |
| Insecure | Confident |
| Mediocre | Elite |
| Amateur | Professional |

### The Identity Transformation (PRIMARY)

> "Identity transformation is the most important part of the framework."
> — Donald Miller

This is the complete arc: Who is your customer NOW vs. Who will they BECOME?

**Example - Luke Skywalker:**
- Before: Lonely, insecure farm boy
- After: Confident Jedi Knight

**Example - Financial Advisor Client:**
- Before: Anxious person uncertain about retirement
- After: Confident retiree enjoying financial freedom

**Example - Fitness Coaching Client:**
- Before: Someone who avoids mirrors
- After: Someone who feels proud in their own skin

### Success Visualization

**Before/After Framework:**
```
BEFORE: [Current state - frustrated person looking at problem]
AFTER: [Future state - happy person experiencing success]
```

Use images, testimonials, and vivid descriptions to make success REAL and TANGIBLE.

### Success Section Template

```yaml
success:
  external:
    - "[Tangible outcome 1]"
    - "[Tangible outcome 2]"
    - "[Tangible outcome 3]"
  internal:
    - "[How they'll feel 1]"
    - "[How they'll feel 2]"
  identity_transformation:
    before: "[Who they are now]"
    after: "[Who they'll become]"
  aspirational_identity: "[The identity they aspire to]"
```

**Source:** [Creativeo](https://www.creativeo.co/post/how-to-nail-the-identity-transformation-piece-of-your-storybrand-brandscript), [Reading Graphics](https://readingraphics.com/book-summary-building-a-storybrand/)

## 10. THE BRANDSCRIPT - COMPLETE TEMPLATE

### What is a BrandScript? (PRIMARY)

The BrandScript is the completed document that captures all seven elements of your brand's story. It serves as:
- The foundation for ALL marketing copy
- A reference for consistent messaging
- A training tool for your team
- A framework for every touchpoint

### BrandScript Access

**Free Tool:** mystorybrand.com
**Physical Templates:** Available at storybrand.com/downloads

### Complete BrandScript Template

```
At [COMPANY NAME] we know that you want to be a [IDENTITY TRANSFORMATION].

In order to do that, you need [CHARACTER WANT].

The problem is [EXTERNAL PROBLEM], which makes you feel [INTERNAL PROBLEM].

We believe [PHILOSOPHICAL PROBLEM].

We understand [EMPATHY], which is why we [AUTHORITY].

Here's how we do it:
1. [PLAN STEP 1]
2. [PLAN STEP 2]
3. [PLAN STEP 3]

So, [DIRECT CTA].

And in the meantime, [TRANSITIONAL CTA].

So you can stop [FAILURE] and instead [SUCCESS].
```

### BrandScript Example: Financial Advisor

```
At Smith Financial we know that you want to be a confident retiree who
never worries about money.

In order to do that, you need a clear, personalized retirement plan.

The problem is you don't know if you're on track, which makes you feel
anxious and overwhelmed about the future.

We believe everyone deserves to enjoy the retirement they've worked
their whole life for.

We understand how scary it is to think about running out of money,
which is why for 20 years we've helped over 500 families retire
with confidence.

Here's how we do it:
1. Schedule your free retirement clarity session
2. We create your personalized retirement roadmap
3. You enjoy retirement knowing you're on track

So, schedule your free session today.

And in the meantime, download our "5 Questions to Ask Before You Retire"
checklist.

So you can stop worrying about whether you'll have enough and instead
enjoy the retirement you deserve.
```

### BrandScript Application Points

Once complete, apply your BrandScript to:
- Website copy (especially homepage)
- Email marketing sequences
- Social media profiles and posts
- Sales presentations
- Elevator pitches
- Advertising copy
- Employee training
- Onboarding materials

## 11. THE ONE-LINER

### What is the One-Liner? (PRIMARY)

The One-Liner is a concise statement (typically 2-4 sentences) that answers: **"What do you do?"**

> "The StoryBrand one-liner is a single statement that you can use to help people realize why they need your product or service."
> — Donald Miller

### One-Liner Formula

```
[PROBLEM] + [SOLUTION] + [SUCCESS]
```

**Detailed:**
1. Talk about the PROBLEM
2. Give the SOLUTION
3. Ramp up the SUCCESS

### One-Liner Template

```
[Identify the problem your customer faces].
[Introduce your solution].
[Describe the success they'll experience].
```

### One-Liner Examples

**Financial Advisor:**
"Most people approaching retirement don't know if they'll have enough money. We create personalized retirement plans that give you confidence. So you can enjoy the retirement you've worked your whole life for."

**IT Services:**
"Technology problems cost businesses thousands in lost productivity. We provide reliable IT support with same-day response. So you can focus on your business, not your computers."

**Real Estate Agent:**
"Finding the right home in this market is overwhelming. We match you with homes that fit your life. So you can find your dream home without the stress."

### Where to Use Your One-Liner

| Location | How to Use |
|----------|------------|
| **Website footer** | Display prominently |
| **Email signature** | Include in every email |
| **Social media bios** | Perfect for limited space |
| **Business cards** | First thing they read |
| **Networking events** | Your verbal introduction |
| **About page** | Opening or closing statement |
| **Elevator pitch** | Memorize and deliver confidently |

**Source:** [Results & Co](https://resultsandco.com.au/blog/storybrand-one-liner-donald-miller), [Hughes Integrated](https://hughesintegrated.com/storybrand-one-liner-examples/)

## 12. WEBSITE WIREFRAME IMPLEMENTATION

### StoryBrand Website Structure (PRIMARY)

Donald Miller provides a specific wireframe for implementing StoryBrand on websites. The key sections:

### Section 1: HEADER (Hero Section)

**Purpose:** Tell customers EXACTLY what you do in 5 seconds.

**Must Include:**
1. **Headline:** Benefit-focused statement (what they GET)
2. **Subheadline:** Brief clarification
3. **Direct CTA button:** Primary action
4. **Success image:** Visual of the outcome

**Rules:**
- Above the fold (no scrolling)
- Clear, not clever
- One message, not multiple
- CTA button visually prominent

**Example:**
```
Headline: "Retire with Confidence"
Subheadline: "We create personalized retirement plans for busy professionals"
CTA Button: "Schedule Your Free Session"
Image: Happy couple enjoying retirement
```

### Section 2: STAKES

**Purpose:** Communicate what's at risk.

**Must Include:**
- 3-4 bullet points of what they stand to lose
- Emotional language
- Connection to their real fears

### Section 3: VALUE PROPOSITION

**Purpose:** Explain what you offer.

**Must Include:**
- List of benefits (not features)
- How their life improves
- What they get

### Section 4: GUIDE

**Purpose:** Establish empathy and authority.

**Must Include:**
- Brief empathy statement
- Authority elements (logos, testimonials, statistics)
- Photo of founder/team (humanizes)

### Section 5: PLAN

**Purpose:** Show the simple path forward.

**Must Include:**
- 3-step process
- Numbered, visual steps
- Clear action at each stage

### Section 6: EXPLANATORY PARAGRAPH

**Purpose:** Provide more detail for those who want it.

**Must Include:**
- Expanded version of your message
- More about your approach
- Additional proof points

### Section 7: VIDEO (Optional)

**Purpose:** Engage visual learners.

**Must Include:**
- Short (under 2 minutes)
- Follows StoryBrand framework
- Clear CTA at end

### Section 8: PRICING/OPTIONS

**Purpose:** Make it easy to choose.

**Must Include:**
- Clear pricing tiers (if applicable)
- Comparison of options
- CTA for each option

### Section 9: JUNK DRAWER

**Purpose:** Everything else (FAQs, additional info).

**Location:** Lower on page, for those who need more.

### Section 10: FOOTER

**Purpose:** Final CTA and contact info.

**Must Include:**
- Repeated Direct CTA
- Contact information
- One-liner

## 13. EMAIL & NURTURE SEQUENCES

### StoryBrand Email Strategy (PRIMARY)

#### Nurture Emails

**Purpose:** Build trust over time with leads who aren't ready to buy.

**Characteristics:**
- Regular (weekly or monthly)
- Valuable content
- Positions you as guide
- Not salesy
- Keeps you top of mind

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge their problem
2. Provide valuable insight/tip
3. Brief mention of how you help
4. Soft CTA (read more, reply, etc.)

#### Sales Emails

**Purpose:** Convert leads to customers.

**Characteristics:**
- Bold and direct
- Clear CTA
- Creates urgency
- Focuses on stakes and success

**The Rule of 7:**
> "The human brain needs to see your brand message 7-8 times before it can be internalized, noticed, and taken action on."
> — StoryBrand

### Email Sequence Template

**After Lead Magnet Download:**

| Day | Email Type | Purpose |
|-----|------------|---------|
| Day 0 | Delivery | Deliver lead magnet |
| Day 2 | Value | Additional helpful content |
| Day 4 | Value | More insight on their problem |
| Day 6 | Story | Testimonial or case study |
| Day 8 | Value | Another helpful tip |
| Day 10 | Soft Pitch | Introduce your solution |
| Day 12 | Sales | Clear CTA to buy |
| Day 14 | Sales | Urgency + final pitch |

**Timing:** Send 2-3 days apart, 7 AM or 11 AM in recipient's timezone.

## 14. COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

### The 10 StoryBrand Pitfalls (PRIMARY)

| # | Mistake | Solution |
|---|---------|----------|
| 1 | Making your brand the hero | Always position customer as hero |
| 2 | Failing to clarify message | Use the 7-part framework |
| 3 | Weak/vague CTAs | Use bold, specific action language |
| 4 | Inconsistent messaging | Apply BrandScript everywhere |
| 5 | Overcomplicating the story | Stick to simple, clear language |
| 6 | Too many words | Edit ruthlessly |
| 7 | Addressing too many problems | Focus on ONE main problem |
| 8 | One-time exercise | Use as ongoing framework |
| 9 | Only positive messaging | Include stakes and failure |
| 10 | Confusing customer journey | Clear path at every step |

### Red Flags in Your Messaging

**Your message is confusing if:**
- You can't explain what you do in 5 seconds
- Customers ask "but what do you actually do?"
- Your team explains your business differently
- Your website visitors bounce quickly
- You're not getting referrals

### The Confusion Test

> "If your message fails what we call the 'grunt test' — meaning a caveman couldn't look at your website and immediately grunt what you offer — you're confusing customers."
> — Donald Miller

## 15. CONSOLIDATED FRAMEWORK

### Principles (Summary)

1. **Customer = Hero** - Your customer is the protagonist
2. **Internal > External** - Sell to emotional needs
3. **Be the Guide** - Demonstrate empathy + authority
4. **Provide a Plan** - Reduce perceived risk
5. **Call to Action** - Be bold and direct
6. **Communicate Stakes** - What they'll lose
7. **Paint Success** - What they'll gain and become

### Complete Process (Step-by-Step)

```
STEP 1: Define Your Character
- Who is your customer?
- What do they WANT?

STEP 2: Identify the Problem (3 Levels)
- External: What's the tangible problem?
- Internal: How does it make them feel?
- Philosophical: Why does this matter?

STEP 3: Position Yourself as Guide
- Write your empathy statement
- Gather your authority elements

STEP 4: Create Your Plan
- Define 3-step process plan
- Define agreement plan (if applicable)
- Name your plans

STEP 5: Define Your CTAs
- Direct CTA: What action do you want?
- Transitional CTA: What lead magnet do you offer?

STEP 6: Articulate the Stakes
- What happens if they don't act?
- What will they lose?

STEP 7: Paint Success
- External success: What results will they see?
- Internal success: How will they feel?
- Identity transformation: Who will they become?

STEP 8: Complete Your BrandScript
- Fill out the complete template
- Review for clarity and consistency

STEP 9: Create Your One-Liner
- Problem + Solution + Success
- Memorize it

STEP 10: Apply to All Marketing
- Website (using wireframe)
- Email sequences
- Social media
- Sales materials
- Employee training
```

### Quality Checklist

- [ ] Customer is positioned as hero (not your brand)
- [ ] Problem is defined at all 3 levels
- [ ] Empathy statement demonstrates understanding
- [ ] Authority elements prove competence
- [ ] Plan is 3 steps or fewer
- [ ] Direct CTA is bold and specific
- [ ] Transitional CTA offers real value
- [ ] Stakes create appropriate urgency
- [ ] Success is vivid and specific
- [ ] Identity transformation is clear
- [ ] BrandScript is complete
- [ ] One-liner answers "what do you do?"
- [ ] Message passes the "grunt test"
- [ ] Consistent across all channels
